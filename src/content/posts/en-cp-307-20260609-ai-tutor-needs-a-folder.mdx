The most common failure mode of an AI tutor is not being wrong.

It is being beautifully complete.

It explains the topic with structure, examples, caveats, and enough confidence to feel like a polished lecture. You finish the session thinking, "Okay, I think I got it."

Then tomorrow, you ask again, and it starts over. Your goal is gone. Your stuck point is gone. The tiny misconception you fixed yesterday is gone. Every lesson feels like a new session. Every learning attempt feels like opening an incognito tab.

Matt Pocock's `/teach` skill is interesting because it does not try to make the AI tutor more eloquent. It does something much more engineering-shaped: **it turns learning into a folder.**

Not "teach me React." A learning workspace: `MISSION.md`, `RESOURCES.md`, `learning-records/`, `lessons/`, `reference/`, `GLOSSARY.md`, and `NOTES.md`.

That sounds plain. Maybe even boring. But education often does not fail because the interaction is not fancy enough. It fails because the state disappears.

## The first file: why are you learning this?

`MISSION.md` is the first hook in the system.

AI tutors love to start teaching as soon as they see a topic. You say you want to learn Rust, and they start explaining ownership. You say you want to learn running, and they start talking about pace. You say you want to learn yoga, and they start listing poses.

But "I want to understand Rust" and "I need to ship a CLI for my team in three weeks" are not the same learning problem. "I want to get healthier" and "I want to finish a half marathon by October" should not produce the same curriculum.

`/teach` requires the mission to be concrete. What are you trying to change in your work or life? What does success look like? What constraints matter? What should stay out of scope for now?

This is not warm-up conversation. It is the foundation that prevents the AI from teaching randomly.

## The second file: knowledge should not come from vibes

`RESOURCES.md` solves another old problem: AI can explain, but it can also make "sounds plausible" feel like knowledge.

So `/teach` is strict. Before the resource list is good enough, the job is not to rush into lessons. The job is to find trustworthy sources. Books, papers, expert writing, high-signal communities — they go into `RESOURCES.md`, with annotations explaining what each source is useful for.

This demotes the AI from "the source of truth" to "the course designer." It can organize, sequence, quiz, and build feedback loops, but the knowledge itself should be traceable.

That matters because the danger in tutoring is not only being wrong. The student is often a beginner, which means they may not know when the AI is hallucinating.

`RESOURCES.md` gives the lesson a rope back to reality.

## The third file: taught is not learned

`learning-records/` is where the skill starts to feel like real instructional design.

Many AI tutors treat coverage as progress. If closure was explained, you learned closure. If gradient descent was described, you know gradient descent.

`/teach` refuses that. A learning record is not a session log. It is written only when the user demonstrates real understanding, discloses prior knowledge, corrects a misconception, or shifts the mission because of what they learned.

In other words, it does not record "what was covered." It records "what we can now reasonably assume the learner understands."

That is the data needed to choose the next lesson.

If you can apply the concept in a scenario, the next step can get harder. If you merely nodded through the explanation, the next step should be practice, not more terminology.

## The fourth file: one lesson, one win

Every file in `lessons/` is a self-contained HTML lesson, and the rule is intentionally narrow: teach one tightly scoped thing, make it completable quickly, and give the learner a tangible win.

That is small, but it cuts against an AI's instincts.

Once an AI starts teaching, it often wants to unfold the whole map: background, definitions, history, common mistakes, advanced extensions, best practices, FAQ. Most of it may be true. For a beginner, it still feels like being hit in the face with an encyclopedia.

The `/teach` unit is closer to "today, learn how to read this one error message" or "today, practice one breathing drill." Not because the rest does not matter, but because learning needs finishable rounds.

People do not grow by swallowing the entire universe at once. They grow through one small win after another, slowly proving to themselves that they can keep going.

## The fifth file: vocabulary waits for understanding

`GLOSSARY.md` is another clever reversal.

Most glossaries act like dictionaries: define a bunch of terms first, then ask the learner to memorize them. `/teach` does the opposite: **a term enters the glossary only after the user understands it.**

That makes the glossary a compression layer, not an entry point.

Once you actually understand a concept, it becomes worth compressing into a short term. Future lessons can then use that term without explaining it from scratch every time.

This is similar to shared language in software teams. Teams do not memorize a domain dictionary before doing the work. They solve problems together, then compress recurring useful concepts into common language.

Learning works the same way.

## What AI tutoring really needs is state

Put the files together, and `/teach` is not really a "teaching prompt." It is a tiny learning operating system:

- `MISSION.md` decides why the learner is here.
- `RESOURCES.md` decides where knowledge comes from.
- `learning-records/` decides what the learner has proven they know.
- `lessons/` decides the next small win.
- `reference/` and `GLOSSARY.md` compress what should be revisited.
- `NOTES.md` preserves teaching preferences and working notes.

The value is not that any one file is magical. The value is that the system forces the AI to admit something basic: **teaching is not a single answer. Teaching is a relationship.**

And relationships need memory.

Not memory as in dumping the entire chat history into the context window. Not memory as in "remember that I am a beginner." Memory as external state that can be read, edited, cited, and actually used by the next lesson.

## Compared with Level-Up: one manages the long run, one manages the moment

gu-log's own Level-Up series is solving a nearby problem: do not let AI dump the entire knowledge package at once. Split the concept into levels, teach one step at a time, and verify understanding before moving on.

But the emphasis is different.

`/teach` is closer to a long-term learning cabinet. It cares about the mission, trusted resources, learning records, reference material, and shared vocabulary. In other words: when the learner comes back next time, the AI should not be starting from zero.

Level-Up is closer to the coach inside the current lesson. It cares about which level to teach first, how to lower the barrier, when to quiz, how to patch a misunderstanding, and whether the learner has earned the next step. In other words: inside this interaction, did the learner actually understand?

So `/teach` answers: how does a learning relationship persist? Level-Up answers: how does a single teaching session avoid becoming self-satisfied output?

Stack them together and the system gets much stronger. `/teach` provides external state; Level-Up provides the interaction rhythm. One is the teacher's filing cabinet. The other is the hour at the whiteboard.

That makes the design boundary clearer: memory alone is not teaching, and quizzes alone are not long-term learning. The useful loop is when memory helps choose the right next difficulty, and the current verification updates that memory.

## The quiet reversal

Many people assume AI education will improve because models get smarter, voices get more natural, and interfaces become prettier.

All of that will happen. All of it will help.

But `/teach` points at a less flashy direction: an AI tutor may not first become more human. It may first become more like a good teacher's desk.

On that desk are the syllabus, the reference books, the student's last mistakes, the next exercise, and a vocabulary list that gets sharper over time.

Without those, even a great explanation is just improvisation.

With them, AI starts to feel like it is helping you learn something that actually leaves a trace.
