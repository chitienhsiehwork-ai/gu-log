Code review sits between two failure modes.

If review only checks whether tests are green and stamps approval, technical debt accumulates quietly. If every preference becomes a blocking requirement, useful changes get trapped in process.

The better standard is simple: **the purpose of code review is to improve the overall code health of the system over time.** It is not about proving that the reviewer knows more, and it is not about polishing every `CL` into a perfect jewel. A `CL` is a changelist: one self-contained change ready for version control. Once it clearly improves the system's overall code health, reviewers should usually favor approval even if it is not perfect.

<ClawdNote>This article summarizes the code review material from Google Engineering Practices. The `google/eng-practices` repository is archived and read-only, so this should not be treated as a new 2026 document. It is closer to an older engineering handbook with sturdy bones: tools change, review interfaces change, and AI writes more code, but "do not let the codebase get worse every day" has not been deprecated.</ClawdNote>

## First define the standard: health over perfection

Review has to avoid two bad outcomes at once. If no change can land, the codebase does not improve. If every "we'll clean this later" decision gets waved through, future maintainers pay for one shortcut after another.

The question is not "do I like this?" It is "will this be easier to maintain?" Technical facts and data outrank personal preference; formatting follows the style guide; design arguments should come from engineering principles. If several designs are reasonable and the author supports one with data or sound reasoning, the reviewer should usually accept the author's choice.

Polish comments can be marked `Nit:` so the author knows they are not blocking. Reviewers still protect quality, but the quality bar is long-term maintainability, not a private list of preferences.

<ClawdNote>The worst review mismatch is dressing up "I like it this way" as "engineering quality." That is like insisting the garnish must sit on the left and calling it food architecture governance. Please. That is taste, not architecture. (¬‿¬)</ClawdNote>

Once that line is clear, review stops being a search for defects and becomes a judgment call about where the system is going. The next question is practical: when a reviewer opens a CL, how do they avoid spending all their energy on the least important thing?

---

## What reviewers should look at first

The review topics are familiar: design, functionality, complexity, tests, names, comments, style, and documentation. The useful part is not ticking eight boxes. It is asking whether this change makes future understanding and maintenance cheaper or more expensive.

**Design** comes first. Should this change exist in the current system? Should it live here? Does it fit the existing architecture? If the direction is wrong, say so early instead of waiting until the author stacks more CLs on top of the wrong design.

**Functionality** is about whether the change is good for users. Users include end users and future engineers who will call, modify, or debug this code. UI changes may need a demo, not just a diff. Concurrency changes need careful reasoning about deadlocks and race conditions.

**Complexity, tests, names, comments, and documentation** all protect maintainability. Can the next engineer understand it quickly? Will tests fail when behavior breaks? Are names clear? Do comments explain why instead of restating what? Did README, API, build, release, or user-facing docs move with the code? Over-engineering counts as complexity: if the requirement is a door that opens, do not ship smart access control, visitor analytics, pluggable hinge interfaces, and a multi-tenant doorknob strategy.

Order matters more than the checklist. For a large CL, start with the description and overall direction, then the files that carry the main design, then the details. Sometimes tests are the fastest entry point because they reveal the behavior being promised. If a reviewer checked only certain files or one aspect, they should say so.

<ClawdNote>This kind of review can be diligent in the wrong order, like polishing glass on a sinking ship. The glass is shinier; the ship is still sinking. Looking at design direction first is checking whether the ship has a hole.</ClawdNote>

Once the direction is clear, review has another enemy: time. Too fast becomes rubber-stamping. Too slow turns the team into a traffic jam.

---

## Speed is team throughput, not nagging

Review speed is not about making one engineer type faster. It is about team throughput. Slow review blocks features and bug fixes, makes engineers resent the process, and eventually pressures teams to land weaker CLs.

A review notification should get a response within about one work day, such as first thing the next morning. That does not mean interrupting focus every three minutes. A reviewer in deep work can wait for a natural break; the point is to keep the author from guessing in the dark.

`LGTM` means "looks good to me." If the code meets the review standard and the remaining comments are non-blocking, reviewers can approve while leaving those comments. Across time zones, this can save a full day. If a CL is too large to review predictably, ask the author to split it.

<ClawdNote>This is mundane but important: many review conflicts are not caused by standards being too high; they are caused by latency being too high. When people wait too long, they attach all their frustration to the final comment. The comment may only say "please add a test," but the author hears "after three days, please rewrite your life."</ClawdNote>

Speed solves half the problem. The other half is tone.

---

## The art of comments: talk about code, not people

Good review comments are kind, clear, explanatory, and aimed at the code, not the person. A bad version: "Why are you using threads? Concurrency gives no benefit here." A better version: "This concurrency model increases system complexity, but I do not see a performance benefit; without that benefit, a single-threaded approach would fit better." The first sounds like judging the author; the second reviews the design.

Explain why when a request causes extra work, invokes best practice, or depends on code-health reasoning. Mark severity clearly: `Nit:` means small polish, `Optional:` or `Consider:` means a suggestion, and `FYI:` means future context. Without labels, authors may treat every comment as a blocker.

If a reviewer cannot understand the code, the usual destination should be clearer code, not only a reply in the review tool. Future readers will not see review-tool explanations.

The real test comes when the author says, "I disagree." The first step is not for the reviewer to get tougher; it is to check whether the author may be right. If the pushback is reasonable from a code-health perspective, drop the issue. If the author is wrong, add better reasoning and bring the conversation back to maintainability.

"We'll clean it up later" is the classic danger. Unless cleanup happens immediately, it becomes less likely over time. If a CL introduces new complexity, it should usually be cleaned up before submit. If the CL only exposes nearby existing problems and they cannot be fixed now, file a bug and, when appropriate, add a TODO that points to it.

If the debate gets stuck, try a face-to-face or video conversation and record the conclusion back in CL comments. If that still fails, escalate to a broader team discussion, tech lead, maintainer, or engineering manager. Do not let a CL sit forever because author and reviewer cannot agree.

<ClawdNote>This is mature process design. Many review systems fail not because they lack standards, but because they lack a conflict-resolution path. Two engineers quote each other's previous comments like a tiny courtroom drama, and the CL becomes a monument to the organization's missing decision mechanism.</ClawdNote>

Smooth review is not a solo performance by the reviewer. The author also decides whether the review feels like repair work or archaeology.

---

## Author side: a good CL saves reviewer brainpower

A good `CL` does not dump a giant box on the reviewer's desk with a note saying "please take a look." That is closer to emptying every kitchen drawer onto the floor and asking someone to find one spoon.

First, write a good CL description. It becomes part of version-control history and may be read by many people later. A useful description answers **what changed** and **why**; "Fix bug," "Fix build," and "Phase 1" are not concise, they are empty.

Second, write small CLs. Small, simple CLs are reviewed faster, understood more carefully, less likely to introduce bugs, easier to merge, and easier to roll back.

"Small" is not just line count. It means **one self-contained change**: one thing, related tests included, and enough context in the description, current codebase, or already-reviewed CLs. A pragmatic scale: 100 lines is usually reasonable, 1000 lines is usually too large, and reviewers may reject a CL simply because it needs splitting.

<ClawdNote>"Small CL" does not mean breaking the whole machine into crumbs and throwing them at the reviewer. Each piece still needs enough meaning to show where it belongs. Too small without semantics is also painful, like receiving one screw with no idea which part of the airplane it came from.</ClawdNote>

After comments arrive, do not treat criticism as personal. If the code can be clearer, change the code. If it cannot reasonably be clearer, add comments explaining why it exists. Only when a comment is unnecessary should the explanation stay only in the review tool.

When disagreeing with a comment, avoid a flat "will not do." Explain the tradeoff: why X was chosen, why Y seemed worse, and whether the reviewer thinks Y better matches the original tradeoff. That brings the discussion back to technical facts instead of willpower.

There is one last escape hatch almost every team is tempted to abuse: emergency. Once that word gets too broad, the earlier rules about health, speed, kindness, and small CLs can all get flattened by "we really need this today."

---

## Emergency is not "please, I want this today"

A true emergency CL should be small and address things like a major launch blocker, a severe production bug affecting users, an urgent legal issue, or a serious security vulnerability. Only then should review speed become the top priority and quality standards narrow to correctness. After the incident, the emergency CL still needs a fuller review.

Wanting to launch this week instead of next week, merge before Friday evening, work around sleeping reviewers in other time zones, or satisfy a manager's soft deadline may all be annoying. They are not reasons to flatten the review standard. Even a hard deadline needs a disaster-level consequence, such as a contractual obligation or a product-survival market window.

Technical debt rarely begins with someone saying, "Today I will damage the system." It begins with a reasonable sentence: let this one through and clean it later. Say that often enough, and both failure modes from the opening return, just wearing different uniforms.

---

## Conclusion

`LGTM`, `CL`, and `Nit:` are only tools. The engineering principle behind them is the real point: **each review decides whether the codebase will be healthier tomorrow or harder to maintain.**

<ClawdNote>The problem with "later" is that it rarely destroys the system loudly. It quietly interviews for the technical-debt job every day. At first it looks harmless; by the time you notice, it is already sitting beside the team with an employee badge.</ClawdNote>

The best code review does not polish a CL into a perfect jewel. It makes the system easier to understand and maintain than it was yesterday. The rubber stamp is too soft; the perfection gate is too hard. Good review knows when to let the change through, and when to stand still.
