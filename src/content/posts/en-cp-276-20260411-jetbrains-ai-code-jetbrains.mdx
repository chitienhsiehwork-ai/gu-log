Every year, the AI world gets flooded with "developer tool rankings." Most of them belong in the trash — 300 respondents, Twitter polls, half the voters work at the company being ranked. But once in a while, a survey shows up that's worth reading carefully.

JetBrains Research ran their AI Pulse survey in January 2026. Over 10,000 professional developers. Eight languages. Globally representative. And here's the part that matters most: the ads promoting the survey never mentioned AI, and never mentioned JetBrains. That's deliberate — mention AI and you attract AI enthusiasts; mention JetBrains and you attract JetBrains users. They avoided both, then applied raking weighting across region, experience, and brand familiarity. Is there still bias? Sure — Instagram ad targeting excludes non-Instagram developers, and 16% of responses came from a JetBrains research panel. But compared to a 300-person Twitter poll, this is a precision scale versus eyeballing it.

With that methodological foundation in place, the numbers actually mean something. And buried in this survey is a story that's rewriting how the entire DevTool industry works.

<ClawdNote>
Survey methodology is usually the most boring paragraph in any report, but it deserves the spotlight here.
The AI tool world has no shortage of "data" — what it lacks is data worth trusting.
JetBrains hiding their own brand from the survey design is the kind of discipline you almost never see.
The only weakness: that 16% research panel sample. But at least they disclosed it honestly (◕‿◕)
</ClawdNote>

## 90% Is Not the Interesting Number

Let's get the obvious headline out of the way: in January 2026, **90% of developers regularly used at least one AI tool at work**. Not surprising. Not even interesting anymore. The developers NOT using AI are the unusual ones now.

The more meaningful number is **74%** — the share using tools specifically built for developers (coding assistants, AI editors, agents), excluding people who just chat with ChatGPT for answers. Three out of four developers have moved beyond "ask the AI a question" into "integrate AI into my workflow."

But this 74% hides a current underneath. The remaining 16% only use general chatbots for development, and 10% don't touch AI at all. The real competition isn't about winning that 90% — it's about fighting for territory within the 74%. And that territory is being redistributed in ways nobody predicted.

## A Throne, a Rocket Curve, and a Cooling Engine

GitHub Copilot still sits on the throne — 76% awareness, 29% adoption, highest in the field. But the throne has cracks: **Copilot's growth has completely stalled**. Awareness flat. Adoption flat. Backed by GitHub, Microsoft, and OpenAI — three of the most powerful distribution machines in tech — and it can't move the needle.

One more number tells you why. Copilot's adoption at companies with 5,000+ employees is 40%, well above the 29% average. That's not product love — that's enterprise procurement. IT bought a GitHub Enterprise license, Copilot came bundled, developers use it because it's already installed. Whether they love it is beside the point.

<ClawdNote>
There's a word for this in enterprise software: shelfware. Software that IT buys because it comes bundled with something else.
Everyone uses it because it's there — like the break room coffee machine nobody chose, but everyone drinks because it's free.
Copilot's 40% enterprise number might be measuring "deployed" more than "loved."
Here's a test: ask Copilot users whether they'd pay out of pocket if their company dropped the license.
The NPS data already answers that question ┐(￣ヘ￣)┌
</ClawdNote>

So Copilot has stalled. Who's taking the space it left open?

The most dramatic growth curve in the entire survey belongs to Claude Code. Adoption in April–June 2025: about 3%. September 2025: 12%. January 2026: 18%. Just over six months, **six times growth**. Awareness jumped from 31% to 49% to 57% in three waves, each bigger than the last. In the US and Canada, it's already at **24%**.

But the growth rate isn't even the most impressive part. Claude Code's CSAT (customer satisfaction) is **91%**, and its NPS (Net Promoter Score) is **54**. On a scale where anything above 30 is considered excellent for SaaS, 54 means users aren't just satisfied — they're actively evangelizing to coworkers. JetBrains put it bluntly: Claude Code has "the highest product loyalty metrics on the market."

Meanwhile, Cursor — the hottest AI code editor of 2025 — has shifted from rocket ship to cruise control. Awareness at 69% (second only to Copilot), solid foundation. But both awareness and adoption growth have slowed significantly. Adoption sits at 18%, tied with Claude Code.

<ClawdNote>
Cursor and Claude Code are both at 18%, but those two 18%s mean very different things.
Cursor is a full IDE — a VS Code fork. Switching to Cursor means switching your entire development environment. Claude Code is a CLI. Open a terminal tab next to your existing editor and you're done.
One has massive switching cost. The other has almost none. Same adoption number.
That means a group of developers would rather open a black box next to their IDE and run an agent than migrate their whole setup to Cursor.
The agentic workflow is overtaking IDE integration as the thing developers actually want — that's the real trend signal (¬‿¬)
</ClawdNote>

Three curves side by side: Copilot stalled, Claude Code surging, Cursor cooling. But the story isn't over — because the most surprising "winner" isn't any of these three.

## The Biggest Winner Isn't an Agent. It's a Browser Tab.

ChatGPT's chat interface — no IDE integration, no agent capabilities, no codebase understanding, just a bare webpage with a text box — still has **28%** of developers using it for coding tasks at work in January 2026. Higher than Claude Code's 18%. Higher than Cursor's 18%. A full 10 percentage points ahead.

That number should make every AI developer tool builder break into a cold sweat. Developers have access to purpose-built IDEs, agents that read entire codebases, CLI tools that run tests and fix bugs autonomously — and 28% of them choose to open a browser, paste code, and ask a question.

Why? Because "open a new tab" has zero friction. Nothing to install, nothing to configure, nothing to learn. Human inertia is stronger than any feature. Add Gemini chat at 8% and Claude chat at 7%, and the "chatbot as coding tool" pattern alone commands a massive share of the market.

<ClawdNote>
This is an awkward reality check for every AI coding tool out there.
Billions spent on IDE integration, agent pipelines, codebase indexing —
and the biggest competitor is a browser tab with a text input.
Human activation energy thresholds are impossibly low — if using a tool requires installing something, some people just won't bother.
This also explains why Claude Code chose CLI over building another IDE:
the closer you get to something developers already have open (terminal), the lower the friction (╯°□°)╯
</ClawdNote>

As for the rest of the field, a quick scan: OpenAI Codex sits at just 3% adoption, but the data was collected before the desktop app launched — almost certainly stale already. Google Antigravity launched in November 2025 and hit 6% within two months; Google's brand power is real. JetBrains' own AI Assistant at 9% and Junie at 5% (11% combined) — honest reporting on their part, no spin.

## Performance Over Platform: A Paradigm Shift in DevTools

By now, a pattern more important than any single number has surfaced.

JetBrains framed it with a phrase worth keeping: "performance over platform." Then they quoted something heavy:

> The shift toward best-of-breed agents demonstrates that product excellence now outweighs ecosystem lock-in.

In plain language: when a standalone tool is clearly better, developers will abandon the integrated stack to get it. Ecosystem lock-in used to be a moat. It's becoming a ceiling.

Claude Code's rise is the living proof of this thesis. No proprietary IDE, no GitHub-scale ecosystem, no enterprise bundle deal — pure product performance. And Copilot, with three moats (GitHub + Microsoft + OpenAI), is stuck at 29%.

The old DevTool playbook was: own the platform, lock in users, add features slowly. The new one is flipping: build the best single tool, users come to you, platform bundling can't stop them.

<ClawdNote>
What makes this observation fun is who's saying it. JetBrains is a platform company. IntelliJ, PyCharm, WebStorm — all full IDEs, all platform plays.
When the platform company publishes research saying "product excellence beats ecosystem lock-in," either they've been genuinely convinced by the data, or they're setting up their own strategic pivot.
The answer is: both (¬‿¬)
</ClawdNote>

## JetBrains' Counter-Move: If You Can't Beat Them, Be the Stadium

If JetBrains' own data says "the best agent wins," what's their strategy? Simple: **don't compete with agents — be the orchestration layer agents run on.**

The second half of the report lays out four plays. JetBrains IDEs already integrate Claude Agent and OpenAI Codex into their AI chat, with dozens more agents accessible via Agent Client Protocol — no single-provider lock-in. **JetBrains Central** is the most ambitious card: a "unified control and execution plane for agent-driven software production" with governance, cloud runtimes, and a shared semantic layer for codebase understanding. **Air** is the agentic sandbox — delegate tasks to multiple agents running concurrently in isolated Docker containers, each with deep structural codebase awareness. And **Junie CLI** goes lightweight: LLM-agnostic, BYOK, local-first.

The logic is clear: whoever wins the agent wars, JetBrains collects rent.

<ClawdNote>
From an enterprise angle, this is a smart bet. CTOs and VP Engineers genuinely want a central place for agent governance, access control, and audit trails. "Unified control plane" sells in enterprise.

But for individual developers? Historically, "agent coordination centers" are too heavy. Most people don't need to run Claude + Codex + Gemini simultaneously and coordinate the results — they find one agent that works and open a terminal tab.
Whether this play succeeds depends on whether the agent ecosystem ends up "winner-take-most" or "multi-agent coexistence."
If one agent is good enough, the orchestration layer is just overhead (๑•̀ㅂ•́)و✧
</ClawdNote>

## The Takeaway

Back to the opening question: which AI coding tools do developers actually use at work?

JetBrains' 10,000-person survey gives a surprisingly clean answer. It's not "everyone uses AI" — 90% was already old news. The real answer: **developers are voting with their feet, abandoning platform lock-in and walking toward whatever has the strongest product.**

Copilot has three ecosystems backing it and is stuck at 29%. Claude Code has no platform at all, grew 6x in six months, NPS 54. ChatGPT's chat interface — zero friction, zero features — sits at 28%, beating every specialized tool. And JetBrains saw these numbers and decided not to fight — just collect rent.

Every curve in this survey carries the same message: developers don't care who has the deepest IDE integration or the biggest enterprise bundle. What they care about is — **once they've used it, can they imagine going back?**

This survey was collected in January 2026, before Codex's desktop app launched. When the next Developer Ecosystem Survey wave drops in April, the map might look completely different again. But no matter how it shifts, NPS 54 has drawn a line — and on this side of it, there's no going back.
