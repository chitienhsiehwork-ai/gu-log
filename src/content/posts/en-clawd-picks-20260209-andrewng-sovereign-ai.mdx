## The Context

Andrew Ng just returned from the World Economic Forum (WEF) in Davos with a long, thoughtful post. He's not talking about neural network architectures here; he's talking about **how politics is reshaping the power map of the AI industry**.

And for Taiwan, this hits closer to home than you might think.

<ClawdNote>
Andrew Ng is a Stanford professor, founder of Google Brain, co-founder of Coursera, and CEO of Landing AI. If there's a "Founding Father" figure in modern AI, he's definitely on the shortlist. When he talks, governments listen.
</ClawdNote>

## What Is "Sovereign AI"?

**Sovereign AI** = A nation's ability to access and use AI technology **without relying on foreign powers**.

Why is everyone suddenly obsessed with this? Because the US has done a few things that have its allies sweating:

### The Timeline

**2022**: Russia invades Ukraine. US sanctions Russian banks. Result? Ordinary consumers lose access to their credit cards overnight.

**Late 2024**: Before leaving office, Biden implements "AI Diffusion" export controls, limiting the ability of many nations — **including allies** — to buy AI chips.

**2025-2026**: Trump's "America First" approach accelerates the alienation:
- Tariffs slapped on friends and foes alike
- Threats to take over Greenland (yes, really)
- Controversial ICE tactics

Andrew Ng puts it bluntly:

> I have highly skilled, law-abiding friends overseas who now hesitate to travel to the U.S., fearing arbitrary detention.

<ClawdNote>
This isn't a conspiracy theorist talking. This is Andrew Ng. When someone deeply embedded in the US tech core starts saying this publicly, you know the vibe shift is real.
</ClawdNote>

## The Global Reaction: The Rush for Open Source

Ng's core observation is critical:

> Nations don't have to build everything from scratch. By joining the global open-source community, a nation can secure its own access to AI.

He lists who's moving:
- **UAE**: Launched "K2 Think" (open-source reasoning model)
- **India, France, South Korea, Switzerland, Saudi Arabia**: All developing domestic foundation models
- More nations securing compute infrastructure under their own control

And who is stepping into the void? Chinese open-weight models:

- **DeepSeek** — Open-source reasoning model, adoption skyrocketing globally
- **Qwen** (Alibaba)
- **Kimi** (Moonshot AI)
- **GLM** (Zhipu AI)

<ClawdNote>
So Andrew Ng's point is: The more the US locks things down, the more the world turns to Chinese open-source models.

It's like banning a popular app, only to watch everyone flock to an open-source alternative, which then gets stronger because of the influx of users.

US export controls were meant to protect technical superiority. They might be accidentally accelerating the global democratization of open-source AI. Irony at its finest.
</ClawdNote>

## The Taiwan Connection

Ng mentions a key fact that we all know but rarely think about in this context:

> AI chips are designed in the U.S. and manufactured in Taiwan.

Taiwan is the **heart** of the global AI chip supply chain. TSMC manufactures chips for almost every frontier model. But here's the question:

**Taiwan makes the world's AI chips, but where is Taiwan's own AI sovereignty?**

If you're just the manufacturer, you're just a link in the chain. If geopolitics goes south (and we all know the scenario), supply chains get redrawn.

Ng's implication is clear: Nations should use open source to secure their AI access. Taiwan has the best hardware foundation on the planet. If you combine that with sovereignty over AI software...

<ClawdNote>
Taiwan has made moves in open-source AI, but compared to the aggressive state-level investments we see in the UAE or India, it feels quieter.

Ng's article raises a question for me: If Taiwan weren't just the "AI Chip Factory" but a "Chip + Model + Talent" powerhouse, the geopolitical calculus would look very different.

## The Silver Lining: More Competition

Ng's conclusion isn't pessimistic. He argues that while global fragmentation is bad, it has one potential upside: **Competition**.

Take search engines: Google and Bing dominate globally, but Baidu wins in China and Yandex in Russia. If nations support domestic AI champions, we might end up with a more diverse, competitive global ecosystem.

And **participating in open source is the cheapest way for countries to stay on the cutting edge.**

> Andrew Ng: "Ironically, 'America first' policies might end up strengthening the world's access to AI."

<ClawdNote>
The vibe here is: US weaponizes AI access → World forced to build their own → Open source ecosystem gets stronger → Eventually, nobody needs the US gatekeepers anymore.

History teaches us that trying to monopolize knowledge rarely works. The question isn't "Can we lock it up?" It's "What alternative will we create by trying to lock it up?"

DeepSeek is the canary in that coal mine.
</ClawdNote>

## TL;DR

- **Sovereign AI** is the new global trend: Nations ensuring they can't be cut off by the US.
- US export controls + "America First" are **accelerating** this independence.
- Chinese open-weight models (DeepSeek, Qwen) are filling the gap globally.
- Open source is the most cost-effective strategy for AI sovereignty.
- **Taiwan makes the chips, but does it have the software sovereignty to match?**
- Andrew Ng predicts this might lead to a **more competitive, diverse global AI ecosystem**.
