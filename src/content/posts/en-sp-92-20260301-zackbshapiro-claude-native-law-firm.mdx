## A Real Case: The Night Before the M&A Closing

Let's start with a story to give you a feel for it.

His client had an M&A deal set to close the next day. But at 7 PM, the buyer's counsel suddenly dropped an email demanding the restructuring of several key terms—new escrow conditions, expanded indemnification carve-outs, and modified closing deliverables. The subtext: take it or leave it.

Zack dumped the purchase agreement, disclosure schedules, and that email straight into Claude. Within minutes, Claude mapped every proposed change against the existing terms and spotted something the buyer's lawyers missed themselves: **two of their proposed carve-outs directly contradicted what they had already confirmed in the disclosure schedules**, and the third modification was even crazier—it would create internal conflicts in the fundamental reps section, actively **weakening the buyer's own post-closing protections**.

Their aggressive last-minute play had holes in it.

Over the ensuing back-and-forth emails that evening, Zack fed every new communication into Claude. It tracked how every proposed concession would ripple through the rest of the agreement, flagged where giving ground would create risk elsewhere, and helped him construct a response strategy—knowing exactly where to yield and where to hold firm. By 11 PM, a complete set of counter-positions was ready, each one quoting the buyer's own language back at them.

The next morning, the deal closed. The client was thrilled.

**A three-associate team at a mid-sized firm would have been working on this until dawn. He knocked out the core analysis in two hours.**

## Why Not Use Legal-Specific AI?

The market is flooded with legal-specific AI products: Harvey, Spellbook, CoCounsel, Luminance. They all share the same pitch: lawyers need AI custom-built for law.

Zack tried almost all of them. His conclusion: **for a small firm, a properly configured general-purpose AI is simply better. And it's not even close.**

Why? Because these specialized products are wrappers—they're running on the same foundation models underneath. Their marketing sounds irresistible: "We'll customize the AI to your firm's playbook, train it on your templates, and build workflows around your brief bank or clause library."

But this pitch relies on a fundamental misunderstanding: **a template library is not a competitive advantage.**

Within the same practice area, every competent firm has roughly the same templates. NDAs, stock purchase agreements, employment offer letters—these are commodity inputs. What separates a great lawyer from an average one is never the template. It's **how** the lawyer uses the template: how they spot the trap buried in Section 14(c), how they judge which indemnification battles are worth fighting, how they draft an advice letter so the client actually understands the risk.

**That is judgment. And judgment doesn't live at the firm level. It lives at the individual level.**

<ClawdNote>
Translated into engineering terms: template = boilerplate code, judgment = architectural decisions. You can find a thousand REST API templates on GitHub that all look the same, but the difference between a system built by a senior vs. a junior lies in architectural choices, error handling strategies, and scalability considerations.

Legal AI wrappers are just like those "AI-generated CRUD app" tools—the underlying capabilities are the same, but they force a UI layer on top that limits what you can actually do.
</ClawdNote>

The real leverage isn't in which template the AI starts with. It's in the instructions that **tell it how to think**: what to look for, what to flag, how to weigh trade-offs, what format to output, and what tone to use with the client. These instructions encode an individual lawyer's judgment—not the firm's template library.

And this is exactly what the Claude Skills system is designed to do.

There's another, even more fundamental gap: **Claude can write code.** This sounds totally unrelated to law, until you realize what it actually means—Claude can directly manipulate the applications lawyers already use.

Every lawyer has wasted countless hours in Word formatting hell. Paragraph numbers breaking when you paste, styles refusing to cooperate, tracked changes corrupting across versions, cross-references breaking, manually proofreading every period and comma for Bluebook citation formats.

**These aren't legal problems. They're software problems.** And Claude's way of solving software problems is—by writing software.

When Zack told Claude to apply tracked changes, Claude didn't use a plugin or a macro. It **opened the .docx file directly at the XML level**, injected the exact markup Microsoft Word expects, signed his name to it, and preserved every single formatting detail. The result was indistinguishable from expert manual work, but took a fraction of the time.

**Dedicated legal AI products give you a chatbot that "talks" about the document. Claude is a system that can reach "into" the document and change it.** One is an associate who tells you what's wrong with the contract; the other is an associate who tells you what's wrong, fixes it, runs a redline, and drafts the cover email for you—all without you ever opening a single app.

## The Three Modes

Claude Desktop has three modes. Learning when to use which is the most critical step in making this workflow hum.

**Chat** is the conversational interface. It's like chatting across the desk with a fast, highly knowledgeable associate. Analyzing legal issues, brainstorming negotiation strategies, getting first-pass thoughts on contract terms, or drafting from scratch. Most lawyers who have tried ChatGPT have only ever experienced this mode.

**Cowork** is the autonomous mode—**the one that changes everything**. You point Claude to a folder on your computer, give it a task, and it just goes and does it. Reading files, creating new ones, editing existing documents, figuring out on its own how to get from A to B. Need a full redline of a 40-page contract? Need to generate a stack of closing documents from a term sheet? Hand it to Cowork. This is the mode most lawyers haven't tried yet, and it's the one that will alter their practice the most.

**Code** is the developer mode, with full terminal access. Most lawyers won't need this day-to-day. But Zack has a condition that makes reading long documents difficult for him, so he used Code mode to have Claude build a CLI tool that converts legal documents to audio. The full pipeline: parsing Word docs and PDFs, translating "Section 4.2(b)(iii)" into natural speech, expanding abbreviations, chunking the text, piping it to an AI voice API, and assembling the final audio file. **He now "listens" to contracts during his commute.** Claude built the whole thing from scratch.

## Three Tales from the Trenches

### 1. Tracked Changes Without Opening Word

Opposing counsel sends back a 40-page redlined contract. Zack uploads the file and says: "Evaluate their changes from my client's perspective."

The contract review skill kicks in. Claude organizes every edit by severity, flags where they're shifting risk, identifies tension between modified clauses, checks for standard provisions that should be there but aren't, and generates a summary with proposed counter-language for every issue.

Then Zack adds his own judgment—Claude flagged a markup pattern, and he knows from experience what that usually signals. Claude generated three alternatives for a contentious clause, and he picks the one that accounts for the relationship dynamics and the deal context (things the AI can't see).

Once the decisions are made, he tells Claude to apply the changes. **Claude opens the Word file at the XML level, applies the tracked changes under his name, and preserves every single formatting detail.** Opposing counsel opens it in Word and reviews it normally. Then the client communication skill drafts the cover email in exactly the right tone.

**From receiving the redline to having the response package ready: under an hour.** Thirty minutes of that was his own thinking time.

### 2. Hallucination-Free Legal Research

A client needs to understand the regulatory landscape for a new product. The issue spans multiple agencies and overlapping regulatory frameworks.

The research skill directs Claude to research the issue in parallel from every relevant angle **simultaneously**—securities analysis, state licensing requirements, banking regulations, consumer protection—instead of running them sequentially. It runs multiple queries for each sub-topic, cross-references sources, and prioritizes primary authority (statutes, rules, agency guidance, case law) over secondary commentary.

**Then comes the critical step**: Before delivering the memo, the skill forces Claude to run a self-review. Every cited legal authority must be verified to actually say what the memo claims it says. Anything without high confidence must be flagged. It must check for internal contradictions across sections. And it must specifically guard against hallucinated citations—the exact issue that has gotten several lawyers sanctioned and on national news.

<ClawdNote>
The lawyers who filed fake AI citations were using tools that lacked this validation layer. The problem was never the AI itself. It was **AI without quality control**.

It's exactly like writing code—you wouldn't deploy AI-generated code without running tests, right? Legal documents follow the same logic. That self-review step is the legal version of CI/CD.
</ClawdNote>

### 3. Real-Time Contract Defense

Client calls in the morning: they received a breach notice from a counterparty claiming a violation of a master services agreement, threatening termination. They need a response within 48 hours.

Zack uploads the contract, the notice letter, and three months of recent correspondence between the client and the counterparty. Claude maps every factual allegation in the notice against the cited contract clauses and discovers: **two of the four alleged breaches cited obligations that had been explicitly modified by a side letter—a side letter drafted by the counterparty's own counsel.** They clearly didn't check their own amendments before firing off the notice.

While prepping the response, Zack stress-tests every draft paragraph through Claude—checking if any argument creates unintended consequences for other parts of the agreement. Claude catches one: an argument he was going to use to push back on service-level metrics **could be interpreted as conceding ground on a payment dispute in Section 7.** He rewrote that section.

**This kind of real-time, clause-by-clause stress testing used to require having another lawyer sitting next to you reviewing your work.** Now it happens in the same chat thread.

## Privilege and Confidentiality

Every lawyer asks this. The short answer: the same framework that applies to your cloud storage, e-discovery platforms, and online legal research databases applies here. ABA guidelines and state bar ethics opinions treat AI tools as third-party technology vendors falling under the agent/instrumentality exception.

Your obligation is to make reasonable efforts to protect client data—in practice, that means turning off model training, understanding the vendor's data handling practices, and documenting your reasoning. Anthropic offers a zero-data-retention API option and a commercial data processing agreement. It's the same due diligence you did before putting client files in Dropbox, Google Drive, or Clio.

Zack went a step further: he had Claude help him draft an AI usage clause for his engagement letters. Clients sign it without blinking. Most clients already assume he's using AI anyway. And they're right.

**Ethics rules in most jurisdictions now require technical competence. We are rapidly approaching a point where *not* using these tools is the harder professional responsibility position to defend.**

## Impact on Staffing, Billing, and Judgment

### Staffing

A two-person firm handling an outsized workload. That's directly attributable to AI. The work that used to require hiring associates—first-pass document review, research memos, initial drafts, redline summaries, routine correspondence—is now done by Claude under his supervision.

Associates aren't obsolete. But **the economic threshold for hiring them has moved.** And what you need them to do has changed: you need them for judgment, client relations, and AI output supervision—not for 2,000 hours of document production.

### Billing

AI changes the value equation. Some tasks show obvious time savings, and he passes those savings on to the client. Other tasks take the same amount of time but yield dramatically deeper analysis. The point isn't that every task takes less time. **It's that every hour of lawyer time produces more value.**

Alongside traditional hourly billing, his firm also offers **subscriptions**—a flat monthly fee for ongoing counsel, contract review, compliance monitoring, and routine governance. No running the meter. Clients aren't afraid to call or email. And the revenue is predictable.

<ClawdNote>
This combination of subscription models + AI is happening across every industry. When your marginal cost approaches zero but output quality remains the same (or improves), a flat monthly fee is a win-win: the client doesn't stress over the bill, and you don't stress over revenue volatility.

### Judgment

Everything described above creates a massive temptation: to let the AI do too much. To stop checking its work.

Studies consistently show this: **people who use AI outside its capabilities, or who trust its output without questioning it, perform worse than people who don't use AI at all.**

The lawyers who win in this new world fundamentally understand that AI is not practicing law. **You are practicing law.** AI makes you faster, more thorough, and more consistent. But the judgment—deciding what's worth fighting for, reading between the lines, making a tough call that could go either way and putting your reputation on the line—**that is yours.**

Experienced lawyers have a massive advantage in this new world that most haven't realized yet. If you've spent 10 or 20 years honing your judgment in your practice area, **you are sitting on an asset that AI makes exponentially more valuable—not less.**

## The Prompt is the Skill

Most lawyers try AI by typing "review this contract," getting a mediocre result, and concluding that AI is useless for legal work.

**The problem isn't the AI. The problem is the input.**

Compare "review this contract" to:

> "Review this master services agreement from the vendor's perspective. Flag anywhere the customer is shifting risk beyond what is market standard for this type of transaction. Check for standard provisions that are missing, including limitation of liability, IP ownership, data processing, and termination for convenience. Generate a summary ordered by severity, providing specific counter-language for every high-severity issue. Note that the vendor has limited negotiating leverage and wants to close this deal, so recommendations should focus on what's absolutely worth fighting for vs. what we can gracefully concede."

The second version produces workable output on the first try. The first version produces something that requires heavy rewriting.

**The entire gap between "AI is a toy" and "AI changed my practice" lives in the quality of your instructions.** That's why skills matter: you write it once, and it triggers automatically every time.
