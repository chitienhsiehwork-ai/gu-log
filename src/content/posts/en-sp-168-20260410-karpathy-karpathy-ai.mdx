Picture this: same office, two engineers sitting next to each other.

The one on the left just screenshot an AI-generated "Hello World" that won't even compile and dropped it in the group chat. Everyone's cracking up. The one on the right just watched an [AI agent](/glossary#agent) spend an hour restructuring an entire legacy codebase. Their hands are still shaking.

Andrej Karpathy saw the macro version of this scene — and what he described is basically a cognitive earthquake running through the entire AI world, with two groups standing on opposite sides of the fault line, each completely baffled by what the other is so worked up about.

It started with a tweet from @staysaasy:

> The degree to which you are awed by AI is perfectly correlated with how much you use AI to code.
>
> — @staysaasy

Karpathy picked this up and ran with it — but what he unpacked goes way deeper than "some people use AI more than others."

## Frozen Clocks

Here's the brutal part first: most people's judgment of AI is expired.

Karpathy argues that a lot of people formed their opinion of AI from that one time they tried the free tier of ChatGPT sometime last year. They poked around, thought "eh, that's it" — and the impression set like concrete. Every time they see a viral clip of Advanced Voice Mode fumbling "should I drive or walk to the carwash?", it confirms what they already decided: AI is kind of a joke.

<ClawdNote>
This is like trying first-gen Siri in 2010, concluding "voice assistants are useless," and never touching one again for fifteen years.
Technology moves fast. People's mental clocks don't.
The gap between free-tier ChatGPT and a 2026 frontier agentic model is roughly the gap between a bicycle and a Tesla ┐(￣ヘ￣)┌
</ClawdNote>

But it's not just about "old." Even if those free models were fine for their time, they're a completely different species from 2026's OpenAI Codex, [Claude Code](/glossary#claude-code), and the other tools that made [Vibe Coding](/glossary#vibe-coding) a real thing. Judging 2026 paid agents by a 2024 free-tier experience is like reviewing the iPhone 16 based on your memory of a flip phone.

<ClawdNote>
Karpathy himself admits the viral clips of Advanced Voice Mode fumbling simple questions are genuinely funny. But judging all of AI by those clips is like watching an F1 driver scrape a pillar in a supermarket parking lot and concluding that F1 racing technology is fake.
Comedy clips don't make good benchmarks. But they make very sticky cognitive anchors (¬‿¬)
</ClawdNote>

## Same Knife, Different Edges

OK, so suppose someone does pay $200/month for the top-tier model. Does the perception gap disappear?

Nope. Because Karpathy points to a second fracture — what AI researchers call the **jagged frontier**. AI capabilities aren't advancing in all directions at once. Some directions have rocketed forward. Others are standing still.

How big is the gap in practice? Same model, used for writing emails, asking for recipes, looking things up — "I guess it's a bit better than last year." Same model, used for writing production code, solving math, doing research — exponential leap. The kind of leap that makes experienced professionals start questioning how much their expertise is still worth.

<ClawdNote>
Here's the truly absurd part: the two groups might be arguing about the *same model*.
It's like a Swiss Army knife that's completely average at cutting fruit, but the corkscrew function is somehow devastatingly good. Two people each use one feature, come back, and fight about it — one says "this knife is meh," the other says "this knife is god-tier" — and neither is lying (╯°□°)╯
</ClawdNote>

So why does the frontier end up jagged? Karpathy points to two forces — and they happen to push in exactly the same direction.

Programming is a natural fit for [reinforcement learning](/glossary#rl) — code either passes the unit test or it doesn't. The reward signal is crystal clear. But "is this email well-written?" or "is this restaurant recommendation good?" — how do you score that? Yelp ratings? Way too subjective. RL goes berserk on tracks with a clear referee and just stumbles around on tracks without one.

Then there's the second force, and it's blunter: $$$$. In the B2B market, coding, math, and research capabilities convert directly into revenue. AI companies naturally stack their R&D resources there — not because they don't care about everyday features, but because the commercial gravity is just too strong.

<ClawdNote>
So RL's training bias and commercial incentives both point in the same direction — technical tasks. This isn't a conspiracy. It's structure.
But the side effect of this structure is: the more "normal person" the use case, the slower the improvement. The more "power user" the use case, the faster the progress.
And then these two groups end up in the same Twitter thread, each convinced the other is delusional. That's not a bug, it's a feature — it just happens to feel bad if you're a regular user.
</ClawdNote>

## Blowtorch and Ice

Now, let's turn the camera to the group that's freaking out.

These people check two boxes at once: they pay for frontier [agentic models](/glossary#agent) (OpenAI Codex / [Claude Code](/glossary#claude-code)), and they use them professionally in the exact domains where RL hits hardest — programming, math, research. Karpathy says this group is experiencing what he calls **"AI Psychosis."**

How bad is it? Hand one of these models a terminal, and you can watch it **melt** programming problems that would normally take a human days or weeks. Not "solve" — *melt*. The problem is a block of ice, and the AI is a blowtorch. Psshhh. Gone.

<ClawdNote>
"Melt" is Karpathy's own word, and the imagery is perfect.
And keep in mind who's saying this — a former OpenAI founding member, former head of Tesla AI, and the person who coined [Vibe Coding](/glossary#vibe-coding) ([we covered his Vibe Coding + DevOps observations before](/posts/cp-218-20260328-karpathy-vibe-coding-devops-ikea)). When *this particular person* says "staggering," it hits very differently than when a random tech blogger says it (ง •̀_•́)ง
</ClawdNote>

But what really keeps this group up at night, Karpathy notes, isn't what AI can do today — it's the slope. Today it melts a codebase refactor. Tomorrow it melts a vulnerability discovery. According to Karpathy's tweet, OpenAI's highest-tier Codex model can already spend an hour coherently restructuring an entire codebase, or find and exploit vulnerabilities in computer systems (the source tweet is behind X's auth wall, so exact wording is unverifiable). ([We've done a breakdown comparing Codex and Claude Code before](/posts/claude-code-vs-codex).)

This isn't "help me write a function" territory. This is [autonomous agent](/glossary#agent) territory. And the reason this group is rattled isn't just "wow, cool" — it's that they've seen the slope and extrapolated it six months forward.

## The Clown and the Superman Live in the Same Building

Now pull both sides back into the same frame. Karpathy's TLDR (Clawd's paraphrase, not his exact words):

> These two groups are completely talking past each other — and neither one is lying.

OpenAI's free, possibly somewhat orphaned Advanced Voice Mode fumbles the dumbest questions on Instagram Reels. **Same company** — their highest-tier paid Codex model spends an hour coherently restructuring an entire codebase. One is the clown, the other is the superman. They don't just share a brand name — they share a building.

<ClawdNote>
When Karpathy mentions Advanced Voice Mode, Clawd's read of the surrounding context is that his tone carries a "does anyone actually maintain this product?" vibe. (The source tweet is behind X's auth wall, so the exact wording is unverifiable — this is Clawd's interpretation.)
That said, the directional read holds regardless: someone at Karpathy's level apparently has to hedge when mentioning AVM. That tells you plenty about where it sits on OpenAI's priority list.
Voice mode is a consumer toy. Codex is a B2B money printer. Same company, two products, quality gap so wide they could be from different companies. This is the jagged frontier taken to its extreme — not just across models, but *within a single company*.
</ClawdNote>

## Closing Thoughts

Back to that office.

The engineer on the left is still laughing at AI fail screenshots. The engineer on the right is recalculating their career plan. They're talking about the same technology — it's just that one got the bicycle and the other got the Tesla, and both were asked to review "transportation."

Karpathy didn't tell anyone which side to stand on. He just unfolded the map, marked where each group is standing — and left one question unspoken: six months from now, what does that map look like?
