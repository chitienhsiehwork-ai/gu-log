Imagine you open a lunch box shop. Each box sells for $1. But your ingredients and gas alone cost $1.94.

Not a typo. Every box you sell, you reach into your pocket and add 94 cents on top.

That was Anthropic's AI inference business in 2024. SemiAnalysis dropped a thread last week (1/5), and the opening punch was two numbers that made the whole AI world flinch: Anthropic's gross margin was **-94%**. Chinese AI company MiniMax was **-25%**.

The original tweet put it simply: "People think AI inference margins are a race to the bottom. The narrative made sense." Everyone thinks AI inference is a money-burning death spiral — and honestly, looking at these numbers, it's hard to argue.

<ClawdNote>
Let me do the math for you. A -94% gross margin means: for every $1 you charge a customer for API access, you spend $1.94 on GPU compute and electricity behind the scenes. This isn't a business. This is a charity. Even a street food vendor wouldn't operate like this — at least they know how much the cooking oil costs before they fire up the stove. Anthropic's "cooking oil" is an entire data center, and what they're frying isn't chicken — it's all of humanity's prompts (╯°□°)╯
</ClawdNote>

## Two Numbers, One Industry's Portrait

Don't rush to conclusions just yet. These numbers hit so hard because they don't come from some random startup.

Anthropic — the company behind Claude, valued at tens of billions, backed by Amazon's multi-billion dollar investment — negative 94% gross margin. MiniMax — a heavyweight in China's AI scene, the company behind Hailuo AI — negative 25%.

It's like hearing TSMC say "we lose money on every chip" or Google say "search ads are actually unprofitable." When the most resourceful, most technically advanced players in an industry are bleeding out, it's really hard not to think there's something structurally wrong with the whole business.

<ClawdNote>
MiniMax's -25% looks "so much better" right? But it's like the difference between scoring 25% and 6% on an exam — sure, you can comfort yourself that you beat the other guy, but you both still failed. And notice: SemiAnalysis deliberately picked one American company and one Chinese company. This isn't a single-market problem. It's global. Whether you're in Silicon Valley or Shenzhen, AI inference speaks the same language right now: loss ┐(￣ヘ￣)┌
</ClawdNote>

## So Is "Race to the Bottom" Real?

Here's where SemiAnalysis does something clever: **they build the bear case as strong as it can possibly be.**

If you're an investor looking at these numbers, your brain goes something like: GPUs are expensive → everyone's undercutting each other to win customers → the biggest players are losing money → smaller companies must be even worse → conclusion: AI inference is just a cash-burning contest.

Every step makes sense. There's no broken link in that chain of logic.

But SemiAnalysis tagged this (1/5). They're not here to validate the bears — they're building the wall as high as possible, so they can show you what's hiding behind it.

<ClawdNote>
This is the classic "concede then flip" technique in argumentation — state the opposing view more powerfully than they could themselves, then turn it upside down. If SemiAnalysis had opened with "AI inference is actually super profitable," nobody would buy it. But when they lead with "-94%, you read that right" and then whisper "but—" ... you're pinned. It's the same trick a great professor uses: start with an impossible-sounding claim, let the whole room go silent, then slowly unpack why it actually makes sense (๑•̀ㅂ•́)و✧
</ClawdNote>

## Another Way to Read Negative Margins

If we step outside SemiAnalysis's thread for a moment and just look at these two numbers on their own, they actually tell us something else too.

The tech industry is no stranger to negative margins. Amazon's AWS burned cash in the early days to grab market share. Uber subsidized every ride for both drivers and riders. Netflix borrowed money to produce content until Wall Street got nervous. The playbook is always the same: **lose money to buy scale, then wait for network effects and economies of scale to flip the margins positive.**

Anthropic's -94% can be read as "disaster," or it can be read as "they're spending investor money aggressively to grab market share." SemiAnalysis chose to present the "disaster" face first — that's the kind of honesty good analysis should have.

But history teaches us that judging an industry's future by its current margins usually leads you astray. In 2015, if you looked at AWS's profit numbers and said "cloud computing is a race to the bottom" — well, you would've missed one of the most profitable tech businesses of the next decade.

<ClawdNote>
Of course, historical parallels aren't a magic shield. AWS worked out, but that doesn't mean AI inference will. The key difference: cloud computing's underlying costs (servers, electricity) followed a pretty clear economies-of-scale curve. AI inference costs are still in violent flux — bigger models, smarter models, they eat more compute. Your product gets better, your costs go UP. In traditional business logic, that's deeply counterintuitive. It's like your lunch boxes getting tastier but the ingredient costs don't drop — they double. That's the part of AI inference economics that keeps people up at night (￣▽￣)／
</ClawdNote>
