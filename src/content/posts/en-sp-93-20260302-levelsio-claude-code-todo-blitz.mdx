## Unsealing the Power Directly: Claude Code Running Naked on the Server

This week, levelsio decided to try something bold: he installed Claude Code directly on his server and **permanently enabled the bypass permissions mode**.
He even wrote an alias script:
`c() { IS_SANDBOX=1 claude --dangerously-skip-permissions "$ @"; }`

<ClawdNote>
Seeing `--dangerously-skip-permissions` running directly on a server might make many traditional software engineers have a heart attack. But in the context of indie hacking, speed is everything. levelsio's philosophy is: instead of spending time on CI/CD and layers of code review, it's better to shorten the feedback loop to "write it, then hit F5 to refresh and see the results immediately." This isn't the best practice, but it's the "fastest practice."
</ClawdNote>

The result? For the first time in his life, his development speed overtook his to-do list.
He went into full beast mode, completely clearing all the tasks on his feature and bug boards. He said he's never developed this fast in his life, and Claude Code barely made any mistakes. Even when it did, they were harmless, minor issues (which is super important to him, since he's mostly working directly on the production server now).

He was already known for being "fast" (since he's always been a solo dev), but no matter how fast he worked, the tickets in his user wish pool would always pile up. But this week, the board was empty!

## The New Bottleneck: Human Creativity and the Context Window

Now, the real bottleneck is no longer "the speed of writing code," but the limits of his own brain.
He found that the speed at which he thinks of new ideas can no longer keep up with the speed at which the AI implements them (though he feels his brain might adapt to this rhythm later on).

Another limit he's about to hit is the "human mental context window." When you have to mentally maintain several projects, dozens of features and bugs simultaneously, while watching the AI work on multiple fronts at once, your brain capacity really isn't enough. He feels he's not far from that limit.

<ClawdNote>
This is very much worth thinking about. When the cost of execution approaches zero, human value will completely shift from "implementation ability" to "requirement definition" and "project architecture." How big your mental context window is dictates how large an AI fleet you can command.
</ClawdNote>

## Trust and Letting Go: The Secret of the Ultimate Fast Feedback Loop

levelsio pointed out a key thing: **The more you let the AI run on its own, the faster it gets.**
Before, he was also slow because he didn't dare to trust the AI and was afraid it would mess up his codebase. But now, he's chosen to just let go.
Just like Andrej Karpathy said, since last December, the models' capabilities have crossed a threshold and have truly reached a level where they "can be used to write code."

He observed that when his friends use Claude Code, their speed is still very slow because they are still checking it line by line. This isn't wrong, of course, but he thinks a better approach is: **Write tests, then let the AI run free and see if it can pass them.**

For levelsio, his "tests" are very scrappy—literally just opening the website and seeing if the new feature works. In 99% of cases, the features are fine. Then he just tells the AI to keep optimizing it.

Because he's running Claude Code directly on the production server, he even saves the time waiting to deploy (even though deploying only took 3 seconds before, it adds up). Now his flow is: Wait for the AI to finish writing → Refresh the webpage → Test. This extremely short feedback loop makes his efficiency go through the roof.

<ClawdNote>
This is the core of the levelsio school of thought: Trust, but verify in production. Traditional software engineering relies on static analysis, unit tests, and staging environments to prevent errors. But he treats "human eyeballs looking at production" as the only integration test. For SaaS products with no legacy baggage, this approach can maximize the LLM's output.
</ClawdNote>
