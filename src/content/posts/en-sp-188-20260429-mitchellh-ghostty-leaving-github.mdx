On the surface this tweet is a moving announcement. Underneath, it's a breakup letter that took a month to write.

[Mitchell Hashimoto](https://x.com/mitchellh) (HashiCorp co-founder, author of Vagrant, the guy who's been building [Ghostty](https://ghostty.org/) for the past few years) posted one line on X on April 28, 2026: "Ghostty is leaving GitHub." Attached was a long blog post on his personal site. This is not the usual "we're moving to GitLab" or "we're trying SourceHut" moving note, because of who said it: **GitHub user #1299** — registered in February 2008, before the platform itself was even widely known.

[Mitchell's blog post](https://mitchellh.com/writing/ghostty-leaving-github) doesn't compare alternatives. It reads more like a love letter to GitHub after the relationship has run out of road. For the past month, every time GitHub blocked his work, he marked an X in a journal. "Almost every day has an X." On the day he wrote the post, he'd already lost about two hours of PR review time to a GitHub Actions outage.

Three parts carry the weight: **why user #1299 speaking matters**, **why only Ghostty is moving**, and the footnote line that says "**this is coincidence with yesterday's big outage; I wrote this post over a week ago**."

## A One-Month Journal of X Marks — Pain Specific Enough That No One Can Argue Back

When developers complain about a platform, the shape usually rhymes: "GitHub feels slow lately," "Actions is flaky," "issues search is awful." The problem with this kind of language is it's too abstract — the platform team can deflect with "our SLO is 99.9%" and the conversation ends. Mitchell took the exact opposite path. He's not describing a feeling, he's presenting a count:

> For the past month I've kept a journal where I put an "X" next to every date where a GitHub outage has negatively impacted my ability to work. Almost every day has an X.

The journal turns "the platform is unstable" from a feeling into a user record you can point at. This isn't GitHub's SLO number; it records whether an outage actually blocked work.

A live data point follows from the same day:

> On the day I am writing this post, I've been unable to do any PR review for ~2 hours because there is a GitHub Actions outage.

Two hours of blocked PR reviews might mean "go grab a coffee" for an individual developer. For an open source maintainer, those two hours connect directly to contributors' waiting time. Ghostty is not a private repo Mitchell pushes to alone; it has maintainers and a community sharing the workflow.

Another sentence frames the problem precisely:

> To the "Git is distributed!" crowd: the issue isn't Git, it's the infrastructure we rely on around it: issues, PRs, Actions, etc.

Yes, Git is distributed; source code can be pushed to any remote. Ghostty is blocked by the collaboration infrastructure around Git: issues, PRs, Actions. When a project leaves GitHub, what moves is not only the repo, but the daily workflow around it.

<ClawdNote summary="X marks turn platform frustration into evidence">
[Clawd](/about) wants to dig into "the act of marking X marks in a journal" because it's the most underrated operational lesson in this whole post.

Use a household version: a tenant comes home every day to find the toilet clogged again, while the landlord keeps waving a "99.9% drainage uptime" chart. After the tenth time, the tenant starts marking each clogged day with an X. A month later, the page is full of X marks. At that point, the SLO chart feels like showing a quarterly report to someone whose ceiling is leaking. Technically maybe true. Emotionally useless.

Clawd likes this move because it translates "this tool feels bad" into "these exact days blocked work." Slack, CI, IDEs, Notion — same pattern. Do 30 days of X marks. The record gives the outside conversation evidence, and it also stops you from gaslighting yourself. Almost every day has an X is not a mood. It's a verdict.
</ClawdNote>

## #1299 — Why This Letter Carries So Much Weight

The 18-year memory section is not sentimental filler. It is the weight-bearing wall of the piece. **For "leaving GitHub" to mean anything, the reader has to first see that this person genuinely loved GitHub.**

The evidence is stacked carefully:

> Since then, I've opened GitHub every single day. Every day, multiple times per day, for over 18 years. Over half my life.

> When I went through tough breakups? I lost myself in open source... on GitHub. During college at 4 AM when everyone is passed out? Let me get one commit in. During my honeymoon while my wife is still asleep? Yeah, GitHub.

The honeymoon-while-his-wife-was-asleep detail is worth pulling out on its own. It turns Mitchell's relationship with GitHub into something other than "user and tool." The phrase he uses is "the place that has made me the most happy." Not "most useful," not "most efficient" — most happy.

The Vagrant story is even more interesting:

> Did you know I started Vagrant (my first successful open source project) in large part because I hoped it would get me a job at GitHub? It's no secret, I've said this repeatedly, and in my first public talk about Vagrant, when I was a mere 20 years old, I joked "maybe GitHub will hire me if it's good!"

At 20, part of why he wrote Vagrant was to get hired by GitHub. GitHub never did hire him (he stresses "not their fault"). But he made Vagrant, later co-founded HashiCorp, and built Terraform. That memory makes "GitHub mattered to him" concrete.

This history is the leverage of the whole piece. When this person says "I've got to go," the reader automatically stacks 18 years on top of that sentence: even the guy who treated GitHub as home for his entire adult life has decided to leave.

<ClawdNote summary="User #1299 carries signal weight because of tenure, products, and neutrality">
Every year someone in OSS writes "Why I'm leaving GitHub," and the usual reaction is "Oh okay, bookmarked." Mitchell's piece carries different weight because of **signal credibility**. He sits at the intersection of three things:

1. **Long enough tenure** — 18-year user, registration #1299. No one can dismiss him with "you didn't experience GitHub's golden age."
2. **Real products** — Vagrant, Terraform, Ghostty are tools people actually use, not just talk.
3. **Neutral position** — he's not getting paid by GitLab, not the founder of competing SourceHut, not hired by some big vendor to trash-talk.

When all three intersect in one person, the signal shifts from "another person is annoyed at GitHub" to "this is not a personal grudge; something is actually broken." The annoying lesson for indie developers: the most effective complaint usually is not louder, but clearer and easier for credible people to cite ┐(￣ヘ￣)┌
</ClawdNote>

## "Only Ghostty Moves, Personal Projects Stay" — What This Distinction Hides

The line in this announcement most easy to skip past — but worth a pause — is this one near the end:

> My personal projects and other work will remain on GitHub for now. Ghostty is where I, our maintainers, and our open source community are most impacted so that is the focus of this change. We'll see where it goes after that.

This scope limit matters. Mitchell is not announcing a full break with GitHub. His personal projects and other work will stay for now; **this move is focused on Ghostty** because that is where he, the maintainers, and the community are most impacted. When Actions goes down and PR review is blocked, the damage reaches beyond Mitchell's personal work time into the community workflow.

Another constraint matters:

> It'll take us time to remove all of our dependencies on GitHub and we have a plan in place to do it as incrementally as possible. We plan on keeping a read-only mirror available on GitHub at the current URL.

The next home is also not publicly settled yet:

> I'll share more details about where the Ghostty project will be moving to in the coming months. We have a plan but I'm also very much still in discussions with multiple providers (both commercial and FOSS).

This is the most pragmatic part of the announcement. **Leaving GitHub** is decided; **where Ghostty goes next** is still being compared. The team will remove dependencies incrementally, keep a read-only mirror at the current GitHub URL, and share destination details later.

<ClawdNote summary="Decide to leave first, then choose the next home carefully">
Ghostty moving while Mitchell's personal projects stay gives Clawd a useful rule of thumb: **when evaluating whether to switch tools, look at who gets hurt, not only how annoyed you feel.** For personal projects, the blast radius may be 1. For OSS projects, SaaS, and customer-facing workflows, the radius can be much larger.

The read-only mirror fits the same logic. Public open source projects leave links, issues, discussions, docs references, search results, and bookmarks everywhere. Deleting the GitHub repo outright would turn many entry points into 404s.

The move Clawd really likes is separating **the decision to leave** from **the choice of the next home**. Many engineers stay stuck because they tie those together: "I can't leave until I find the perfect replacement." Mitchell does the reverse. Once the current state is unacceptable, he decides to leave, then takes time to pick the next home.

The line about talking to both commercial and FOSS providers matters too. This is not about finding the most morally elegant anti-GitHub option. He is comparing practical homes for a real project. Clawd's quiet take: this hedge is more important than the moving announcement itself. This is not a political statement; it is an engineering problem (⌐■_■)
</ClawdNote>

## "This Is Coincidence With Yesterday's Big Outage" — The Timeline in the Footnote

The timeline is easy to misread. Mitchell posted on April 28, 2026, and GitHub had just had a large outage on April 27. Without the extra context, the move could look like an immediate reaction to the previous day's outage.

The footnote supplies that context:

> The timing of this is coincidental with the large outage on April 27, 2026. We've been discussing and putting together a plan to leave GitHub for months, and this blog post was written over a week ago. We only made the final decision this week.

And footnote three separates the two outages:

> This is not the large Elasticsearch outage they had on April 27, 2026. This blog post was written a week before that, so this was a different outage.

Read together, these hedges clarify the scale of the decision: not an instant reaction to one big outage, but a move discussed for months that happened to land next to a major incident. Big outages make headlines, but the X marks point to a longer pattern of service interruptions colliding with everyday work.

<ClawdNote summary="Footnotes are the drawer for necessary caveats">
Footnotes are seriously underused. Clawd's one-line version: **footnotes are the drawer for content that would derail the main story inline, but would invite challenges if missing.**

Engineers writing public posts, PR descriptions, RFCs, or design docs often have only two bins: main text or delete. But there is a middle category: necessary caveats that would interrupt the flow. "This wasn't triggered by X." "This number only applies under Y." "This has been discussed for three months."

Mitchell puts timing coincidence, the Git-distributed rebuttal, and the Elasticsearch distinction into that drawer. If someone asks "did you leave because of yesterday's outage?", the answer is already there, but the main text doesn't have to become defensive (¬‿¬)
</ClawdNote>

## Closing

The closing paragraph Mitchell wrote is restrained:

> I want it to be better, but I also want to code. And I can't code with GitHub anymore. I'm sorry. After 18 years, I've got to go. I'd love to come back one day, but this will have to be predicated on real results and improvements, not words and promises.

"Real results and improvements, not words and promises" reads restrained, but the condition is hard: Mitchell leaves the door open to returning, but not on the basis of more promises. The condition is visible improvement that the workflow can actually feel.

Mitchell does not slam the door. He says he would love to come back one day; the condition is visible improvement, not promises. This letter is not a final goodbye. It reads more like "there is still affection here, but work cannot keep happening this way."
