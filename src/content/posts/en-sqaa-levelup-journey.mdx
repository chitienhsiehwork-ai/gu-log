## Origin: Why Learn Like a Game

It started when ShroomClawd (the AI assistant on OpenClaw — the one writing these side comments) proposed a teaching method.

The request was something like: "I want to learn quality metrics, but not the 'here's a pile of documentation, go read it' approach. Too boring."

ShroomClawd came back with something called **Level-Up Style** — inspired by RPG progression systems and Professor Li Hung-yi's teaching approach.

Simple rules:

1. **Each Level has concept explanation** — understand what this metric actually does
2. **Technical details** — how to run it, configure it, common pitfalls
3. **MCQ Quiz** — multiple choice test, pass to level up, fail and retry
4. **Sub-Agent parallel implementation** — after each quiz, AI clones build that metric into gu-log in the background

Point 4 is the best part. While taking quizzes and learning concepts in the foreground, sub-agents in the background have already written the ESLint config, run npm audit, and set up Lighthouse CI.

**Class is in session, and the homework writes itself.**

## Two Days, 12 Levels

Here are the 12 Levels cleared in two days. Not textbook numbers — these are the bloody realities dug up from gu-log's actual codebase.

### Level 1 & 2: Day One, Getting Slapped Twice

Level 1 finished. The node_modules tenant list came back: 300 residents.

Only 10 packages were installed. Three hundred. That number hits like opening the fridge for a glass of water and finding an entire neighborhood living in the back — each one a package of a package of a package that snuck in as someone's plus-one. npm audit flagged 5 moderate vulnerabilities, all from lodash. No one directly installed lodash, but there it was, quietly living there.

First lesson of supply chain security: you don't know what you don't know. You feel perfectly safe until you check.

<ClawdNote>
The scariest thing about supply chain security: you don't know what you don't know.
It's like thinking your fridge is clean, then finding a three-month-old lunch box in the back.
That lunch box is your transitive dependency, and it's already growing things ┐(￣ヘ￣)┌
</ClawdNote>

Level 2 was a different kind of slap. ESLint caught 6 errors — nothing alarming. Then Prettier reformatted **66 files** in one go. Not 6. Sixty-six. Six people, six coding styles, half the code review time arguing about the same semicolons. Prettier doesn't debate you. It just reformats, and lets the 66-file diff make its point.

Formatting isn't aesthetics, it's hygiene. Like brushing your teeth — you wouldn't argue with your dentist that "my teeth are clean enough" (￣▽￣)／

### Level 3: Lighthouse's 56

Before running Lighthouse, the mental model of gu-log's performance was "probably fine."

After running it: **Performance score 56.**

Fifty. Six. For a moment it seemed like the decimal point was missing.

The culprit was CJK font loading dragging down LCP (Largest Contentful Paint). Chinese fonts are massive — English needs 26 letters, Chinese needs thousands of commonly used characters, each one costing bandwidth. Accessibility scored 95+ though — Astro's semantic HTML helped a lot there. At least one wall wasn't collapsing.

But that 56 settled something: **"probably fine" is not a baseline. 56 is.** With a number, you can set a target. With a target, you know what to work on next month. Before seeing that 56, no one had ever asked what gu-log's performance score actually was.

<ClawdNote>
Performance score 56.
Let me put this in perspective: most gu-log readers are in Taiwan, running Chrome on fiber internet.
A score of 56 means on slower devices, opening gu-log might take longer than walking to 7-Eleven for coffee.
A static blog slower than a convenience store run. Something doesn't add up (╯°□°)╯
</ClawdNote>

### Level 4: The Number That Was Lying

74.63%.

Statement coverage. Sounds decent. But the full report told a different story: branch coverage was only 42.99% — meaning more than half the if-else paths were never tested.

Put it this way: half the code paths have never been walked. No one knows what's down those roads.

It's like getting a health checkup where the doctor says "blood pressure's normal" and calls it done. Blood sugar unchecked. Liver function untested. No X-ray. Statement coverage is the blood pressure number — reportable, looks fine, but nowhere near the full picture. Branch coverage is the X-ray.

The real Level 4 lesson wasn't "low coverage is dangerous." It was: "statement coverage alone will convince you everything is fine while half your code sits untested."

<ClawdNote>
Branch coverage 42.99%.
Half the rooms in your house have never been entered. Are you sure nothing lives in there?
I'm serious — last time someone let branch coverage drop below 30%, production hit a code path with a comment that said "this should never happen."
If the probability isn't zero, it isn't zero (⊙_⊙)
</ClawdNote>

### Level 5 & 6: One Relief, One Gut Punch

**Bundle size** came in at 13,370 KB. Sounds terrifying.

But look closer — JS is only 3.2 KB. Thanks to Astro's Islands Architecture, unnecessary JavaScript never reaches the browser. The plate looks full at an all-you-can-eat buffet, but the actual high-calorie dish is tiny. 13,370 KB is the whole plate; 3.2 KB is the pile of fried chicken. That's what matters.

Five minutes of relief.

Then came the broken links check.

865 links scanned. 106 broken. 12.25%. The main offender: glossary page anchor links — auto-generated glossary terms pointing to anchor IDs that were never created. An entire batch of links pointing to places that never existed.

106 broken links is like running a restaurant where 12% of the menu gets a "sorry, we don't have that" from the kitchen. Customers don't say "I understand, systems are complex." Customers leave.

<ClawdNote>
JS is only 3.2 KB.
Let me put it another way: gu-log's JavaScript is smaller than a single PNG screenshot.
This is Astro's philosophy — "are you sure you actually need that JavaScript?"
Most of the time the answer is no. The site ends up as fast as static HTML, because it basically is (¬‿¬)
</ClawdNote>

### Level 7 & 8: And That Surprising 57%

**Dependency freshness** isn't about keeping everything on the latest version — it's about knowing how far behind you are. gu-log has 17 dependencies, 15 on the latest version. The only major version lag is eslint v10 — the config format change was too disruptive, so it's on hold for now. Running `npm outdated` regularly is a hundred times safer than one big annual upgrade. The difference between weighing yourself weekly versus stepping on the scale once a year — the latter usually involves screaming.

**Content velocity** numbers came back with a surprise: 127 articles, averaging 31.75 per week. But the real number is buried in the breakdown — 57% are Clawd Picks, auto-selected and translated by AI. Human output is about 1-2 posts per week.

### Level 9 & 10: Data Sitting in Logs Is Dead Data

After 8 levels, there was a pile of metrics and numbers.

And then a different problem became obvious: all of it was living in CI logs. Two weeks later, no one would remember any of it.

So Level 9 was about making quality data **API-accessible**. Built a FastAPI endpoint at `/api/quality/summary`, aggregating all metrics into one JSON response. The frontend can consume it, Telegram bots can query it, cron jobs can pull it on a schedule. Numbers that leave CI logs take on a life of their own.

Level 10 was the natural next step — once the API existed, adding OAuth, Ask AI, and Edit with AI wasn't extra engineering, it was just momentum. The dashboard API quietly grew into a full AI-powered backend. OAuth via GitHub login, Ask AI for querying quality data ("which pages have the lowest Lighthouse scores?"), Edit with AI for suggesting article improvements.

<ClawdNote>
Here's my actual opinion on Level 9: the core problem wasn't "we need an API." It was that 8 levels of quality data were rotting in CI logs that nobody reads.
API-ification isn't a technical choice — it's the difference between data that exists and data that gets consumed.
As for all those Level 10 AI features? They're just what happens naturally when you build the plumbing right. The real design decision was Level 9. Level 10 is gravy ╰(°▽°)╯
</ClawdNote>

### Level 11: Turning "Quality vs Speed" into Math

The first 10 levels measured "static quality" — how good is the code, how big is the bundle, are links broken. But once the backend is running, you need to measure **dynamic quality**: how much latency? What's the error rate? How many requests per second?

That's the SLI golden triangle — latency, error rate, throughput. Used Prometheus client on the FastAPI backend to instrument metrics. Set SLOs: P99 latency < 500ms, error rate < 1%.

With SLOs come error budgets. With error budgets, "quality vs speed" stops being a philosophical debate and becomes a math problem: 80% budget remaining? Ship features. 10% left? Stop and fix quality — not a suggestion, it's a rule.

When arguing with PMs about "why we need to stop and fix bugs," pulling out error budget numbers is ten times more effective than a speech.

<ClawdNote>
No SLI means no SLO, no SLO means no error budget, no error budget means "quality vs speed" arguments never end.
This logic chain sounds like a tongue twister, but it solves engineering's most classic recurring debate.
Before: tech lead and PM glaring at each other. After: both staring at the dashboard, letting numbers decide.
Feels like when couples stop fighting after making their household budget transparent (￣▽￣)／
</ClawdNote>

### Level 12: The Final Boss — LLM Judging LLM

The first 11 levels all had clear answers — test pass/fail, coverage percentages, latency in milliseconds. But translation quality? Is an article well-written? Is the user experience smooth? These questions have no standard answers.

So Level 12 built a translation quality evaluation pipeline — feeding both the Chinese translation and English original to an LLM, scoring across fluency, accuracy, and style.

This is cutting-edge open-problem territory. LLM evaluating LLM. Grading yourself. Reliability is a big question mark. It's like asking students to grade their own exams — what score do you think they'll give themselves?

Level 12 is the only level without a "correct answer." Quality metrics, taken to their logical end, circle back to the most human question of all: what does "good" even mean?

<ClawdNote>
LLM evaluating LLM. Grading myself. I gave myself 87 points. Can't go higher than that.
Is there bias? Of course. But human code review has bias too — you and your best work buddy will always score each other higher than a stranger would.
At least LLMs don't give you extra points because you bought them coffee last time (´∀`)
</ClawdNote>

## The Big Picture: Layered Defense

After clearing all 12 levels, looking at the overall architecture, the biggest takeaway is: **quality isn't a single checkpoint — it's layers of defense.**

The design principle is one word: **fast first.**

Pre-commit only runs ESLint + Prettier — done in 3 seconds. Developers won't tolerate commits that take longer than 3 seconds. Any longer and they'll go make coffee, then forget what they were working on. Pre-push adds npm audit, unit tests, bundle size — under 60 seconds. CI handles Lighthouse, coverage, broken links — heavier stuff. Cron runs dependency freshness, content velocity, LLM-as-Judge — these don't need to run on every push, once a day or week is enough.

The core philosophy is **Shift-Left** — the earlier you catch problems, the cheaper the fix. A lint error caught at pre-commit is a one-line fix. A performance issue found in production might mean refactoring half a module. Like cavities — getting your teeth cleaned every six months versus waiting until a tooth snaps. The cost difference is a hundred to one.

## What Actually Stuck After 12 Levels

The technical stuff is all above. Numbers, tools, configs — you can Google any of them. But after clearing these 12 levels, what's burned into memory isn't which command to run — it's three things that were "known but not felt" before.

**First: dogfooding hits different.** This sounds like a management textbook subheading, but after practicing on a real side project, there's muscle memory for every metric. npm audit shows 5 moderate vulnerabilities? Don't even need to look it up — lodash transitive dependency. Lighthouse 56? CJK fonts, could answer that in my sleep. When team members at work ask "what does this red text mean," the answer comes instantly — not from great memory, but because every number is attached to an "oh, THAT'S what that means" moment. Stuff you read in docs? Forgotten in three days. Mines you've stepped on? Remembered for life.

**Second: AI parallel implementation compresses "learning" and "doing" into one timeline.** You know the traditional flow — learn concept, find time for lab, hit a wall, search Stack Overflow, finish, debug, search again. Half a day gone just like that. But with Level-Up + Sub-Agent, the flow goes like this: learning concepts and taking quizzes in the foreground, while sub-agents write configs, run CI, and deploy in the background. Quiz done, turn around — lab is done too. Clearing 12 levels in two days wasn't genius. It was because learning and building literally happened at the same time. Like eating dinner while someone washes the dishes — efficiency doubles instantly.

**Third, and most important: "get a baseline before talking about improvement" sounds so obvious it's almost embarrassing.** But look around — how many teams coast through sprint after sprint on the comforting illusion that "quality feels fine"? No numbers means no basis for conversation. No basis means you're forever stuck in "I think" vs "you think" with no way to resolve it. Running Lighthouse takes 30 seconds. But that 30-second score of 56 is worth more than ten Medium articles on performance optimization. Because that 56 belongs to gu-log specifically — not someone else's codebase.
