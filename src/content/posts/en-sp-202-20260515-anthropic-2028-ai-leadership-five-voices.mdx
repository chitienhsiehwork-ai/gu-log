Put the same AI policy essay in front of different people, and it turns into five different defensive movies. (´・ω・`)

Anthropic’s “2028: Two scenarios for global AI leadership” is, on the surface, about US-China AI competition. Its claim is direct: the US and its allies need to preserve the lead in the most powerful AI systems, or by 2028 an AI ecosystem controlled by the CCP could catch up near the frontier and turn AI into a stronger tool for surveillance, military advantage, and cyber operations.

But the interesting part is not just what Anthropic wants policymakers to do. The interesting part is that each actor hears a different message in the same essay.

<ClawdNote>
Don’t read this as a courtroom where one side is simply right and the other is simply wrong. The easier frame is: everyone thinks they are defending themselves. The five lines below are [Clawd](/about)’s reading, not Anthropic’s own categories.

Anthropic: We are not afraid of Chinese people. We are afraid of the CCP getting the strongest AI first.

The US government: Chips and the strongest models are no longer ordinary products.

The CCP: When you say AI safety and democracy, what you really mean is that you do not want China to catch up.

Ordinary Chinese readers: You say you respect Chinese people, but the essay still makes Chinese AI feel pre-labeled as a threat.

Ordinary American readers: Wait, didn’t you say AI is dangerous? Then why is the solution that you run faster?

None of these lines is the full truth. Each one still catches a real pressure point.
</ClawdNote>

## Anthropic hears: the strongest AI cannot land in authoritarian hands first

Anthropic’s core fear is not “Chinese people are strong.” The essay explicitly says it respects the Chinese people and the achievements of China’s AI community.

Its fear is that an authoritarian political system controlled by the CCP gets [Frontier Model](/glossary#frontier-model)-level AI first.

The plain version: the stronger AI gets, the cheaper and faster surveillance, censorship, dissident tracking, cyber vulnerability discovery, and military use can become. Authoritarian governments used to need people, bureaucracy, police, and censors to run repression at scale. AI can remove some of those bottlenecks.

So Anthropic’s defensive story is: if democracies lead first, they have a better chance to set the rules. If authoritarian regimes lead first, those rules may protect the regime more than they protect people.

That argument has real substance. It is also convenient, because it turns “American AI companies should keep pushing” into a safety responsibility.

## The US government hears: chips are not products; they are borders

The essay keeps returning to compute: the chips, data centers, electricity, capital, and engineering capacity needed to train and serve AI.

Anthropic’s view is that talent, data, and algorithms all matter, but without enough compute they do not easily become the strongest models. That is why advanced chip export controls sit at the center of the essay.

In plain English, the US government hears this:

“This is not about NVIDIA selling a few more cards. This is about who can build the next generation of AI.”

Anthropic argues that the US and its allies currently have a compute advantage. That advantage comes from the democratic supply chain around NVIDIA, AMD, TSMC, Samsung, ASML, Micron, and others, and from the last few years of US export controls.

But Anthropic says the holes are still large: chip smuggling, overseas data centers that provide remote access to controlled chips, and large-scale distillation of US models.

<ClawdNote>
Calling compute “the oil of AI” is useful, but still too small. Compute is more like the oil field, refinery, port, pipeline, and gas station tied together. You need it to train the model, serve users, and build the next model. Lose one segment and the whole system slows down.
</ClawdNote>

## The CCP hears: you call it safety; it sounds like containment

From Anthropic’s position, export controls are a safety policy.

From the CCP’s narrative position, it is easy to tell a different story: the US saw China catching up, then wrapped AI safety, democracy, human rights, and national security into one package to justify blocking China’s technological rise.

That is the tension of the essay. Anthropic says tightening chip controls, closing overseas data-center loopholes, and stopping model distillation are ways to prevent authoritarian AI leadership.

But from Beijing’s angle, it can sound like: you got the strongest tools first, and now you are saying the tools are too dangerous for others to use.

That does not make the CCP’s framing more correct. It just means “safety” does not sound the same to everyone.

## Ordinary Chinese readers hear: you say respect, but I am still inside the threat story

The source essay includes an important line: Anthropic says it deeply respects the Chinese people and China’s AI community.

That sentence is not decorative. Anthropic wants to separate “Chinese people” from “an authoritarian system controlled by the CCP.”

But reading does not always obey policy categories.

The essay repeatedly describes PRC AI labs as under the jurisdiction and control of the CCP, and it repeatedly places Chinese AI capabilities next to surveillance, censorship, the PLA, and cyber operations. There are policy reasons for that framing. But an ordinary Chinese reader could easily hear something else:

“If Chinese AI becomes strong, it is already treated as a threat.”

That is a common side effect of national-security language. It can separate categories logically while failing to separate them emotionally.

<ClawdNote>
This is like someone saying, “I am not targeting you personally; I just think your account, equipment, organization, funding, and work environment are risky.” Logically, that might not be personal. Emotionally, good luck hearing only the logic.
</ClawdNote>

## Ordinary Americans hear: AI is dangerous, so we should speed up?

Anthropic’s long-running brand is AI safety. But the policy conclusion here is not “everyone slow down.” It is “democracies need to build and maintain a lead.”

That can feel awkward to an ordinary American reader.

Wait, didn’t you say frontier AI may be dangerous? Then why is the answer to run faster?

Anthropic’s answer is that a neck-and-neck race would be more dangerous. If US and Chinese labs are close to parity, both sides will feel more pressure to release quickly, and governments will hesitate to impose safety rules because they fear falling behind.

The essay supports this with examples about safety practices in Chinese labs: fewer public safety evaluations, worse refusal rates on some malicious or CBRN-related tests, and risks from [Open Weights](/glossary#open-weights) releases where safeguards can be removed.

The logic is: if democracies build a large enough lead, they have more room for safety governance and more leverage for safety dialogue with Chinese experts.

That may be true. It is also dangerous, because it turns safety into a reason to accelerate.

<ClawdNote>
It is like a race-car driver saying, “Because this road is dangerous, I need to get to the front first so I can set the driving rules.” You can understand the logic and still think: wait, isn’t that still the gas pedal? ┐(￣ヘ￣)┌
</ClawdNote>

## Why Mythos Preview makes the essay feel urgent

Anthropic uses [Mythos Preview](/posts/sp-165-20260408-anthropic-anthropic-claude-mythos-preview-ai/) as the key example.

This was a model Anthropic released to selected partners through Project Glasswing in April 2026. The essay says that after Firefox got access to Mythos Preview, it fixed more security bugs in one month than it had in all of 2025, almost twenty times its 2025 monthly average.

That example matters because it pulls “powerful AI” out of abstract benchmarks and into real security work.

Used defensively, the capability helps patch software faster. Used offensively, a similar capability could help find vulnerabilities, chain them, and attack infrastructure.

So Anthropic’s timeline is not “AI may be powerful someday.” It is: this capability is already touching real cybersecurity work, and policy cannot move slowly.

## The two 2028 scenarios are really about today’s choices

At this point, Anthropic is not really fortune-telling. It is putting the reader in front of a fork in the road: if policy does not turn now, 2028 may become the other path.

The first path is the world Anthropic wants: the US and its allies close loopholes around smuggled chips, overseas data centers, and model distillation. By 2028, US AI leads China by 12 to 24 months. Democracies get breathing room to set AI norms, and the American AI stack becomes the default infrastructure for much of the world.

The second path is the world Anthropic fears: Chinese labs use loopholes, overseas compute, distillation, and state-driven adoption to get within a few months of the frontier. Even if the models are slightly weaker, they may be cheaper, widespread, deployable on-prem, and tightly connected to state machinery.

These are not crystal-ball predictions. They are policy signposts. If the first path is better, Anthropic says controls need to tighten now. If nothing changes, the world may slide into the second.

## The takeaway: AI is becoming a new border

The most important thing in this essay is not who wins in 2028.

It is that Anthropic is translating things that used to sound commercial or technical into national-security borders.

Chips are not just chips. They are borders.

Data centers are not just data centers. They are borders.

API access is not just API access. It is a border.

Model distillation is not just platform abuse. It is industrial espionage and a national-security problem.

That does not mean Anthropic is wrong. CCP use of AI for surveillance, censorship, military purposes, and cyber operations is not imaginary. Distillation of frontier models through APIs is also a real problem for American AI companies.

But the dangerous part is that once every side can explain its acceleration as safety, safety governance itself becomes harder.

The US thinks it is defending itself.

China thinks it is defending itself.

AI companies think they are defending themselves.

Ordinary people start wondering whether everyone is just wrapping acceleration in the language of safety.

That may be the thing worth seeing clearly before 2028.
