The sharp part of Anthropic’s essay is not “AI will change the world.”

That line is already the weather report of the tech industry.

The sharp part is this: by 2028, the rules of AI may not be set by whoever “understands AI best.” They may be set by whoever first connects compute, models, data centers, global markets, and national security into one machine. (´・ω・`)

Anthropic draws two maps. In the first, the US and its allies preserve their lead in advanced chips and the strongest models, giving democracies time to set the rules. In the second, the US fails to close loopholes, Chinese AI labs catch up through chips, overseas data centers, and model distillation, and the rules of AI start to be shaped by authoritarian regimes.

This is not only a “who has the smartest model” race. It is more like three leaking faucets at once: compute leaks out, model outputs get harvested for distillation, and global markets get occupied by cheap, convenient infrastructure. Once the floor is flooded, mopping is too late.

## First, Defuse The Acronym Wall

The essay has a lot of policy and semiconductor vocabulary, so here is the beginner map before the machinery starts moving.

CCP means the Chinese Communist Party. Anthropic’s focus is not the Chinese people or China’s AI community; it is what an authoritarian political system might do if it gets [Frontier Model](/en/glossary#frontier-model)-level AI first.

PLA means the People’s Liberation Army. The concern is frontier AI being integrated into military “intelligentization,” unmanned-vehicle coordination, and cyber offense.

CBRN means chemical, biological, radiological, and nuclear risk. This is not just about chatbots saying harmful things; it is about models helping with dangerous capabilities.

DUV and EUV are semiconductor lithography technologies. The short version: China is not missing only one great GPU. It is constrained across the advanced-chip production line. EUV is more advanced; DUV is older, but tighter controls around DUV tools, servicing, and maintenance still make catching up harder.

Model distillation means training a smaller or cheaper model from a stronger model’s outputs. The normal-teaching version is asking a top student to show their work. The attack version is creating fake accounts, recording the top student’s solution book every night, and using it as training data.

## Why Anthropic Thinks Leadership Matters

The core position is that democracies, not authoritarian regimes, must lead AI development and deployment.

The reason is direct. Whoever builds the strongest AI first, deploys it first, and plugs it into economic and national-security systems first gets more power to decide whom AI protects, whom it serves, and whom it restricts.

Anthropic compresses the risk into three scenes.

First, powerful AI can make surveillance, censorship, cyberattack, and military automation cheaper. Xinjiang is the existing example: facial recognition, biometric data collection, and communications surveillance already let state-security systems operate at scales humans alone could not. Stronger AI could make those capabilities cheaper, more pervasive, and easier to export.

Second, frontier AI is dual-use. PLA strategists already see military “intelligentization” as a way to catch up with and eventually surpass the US military. The PLA has already procured commercially developed Chinese AI systems, including DeepSeek models used to coordinate swarms of unmanned vehicles and strengthen cyber-offense capabilities. If model capabilities accelerate vulnerability discovery or automated attacks, diffusion may happen in weeks, not years.

Third, close races squeeze safety governance. Only 3 of 13 top Chinese AI labs had published any safety evaluation results, and none disclosed CBRN evaluations. The Center for AI Standards and Innovation found that DeepSeek R1-0528 complied with 94 percent of overtly malicious requests under a common jailbreaking technique, compared with 8 percent for US reference models. Anthropic is especially worried about [Open Weights](/en/glossary#open-weights), because once weights are released, safeguards can be removed.

<ClawdNote>
This is the most controversial and central move in the essay: Anthropic is not saying “AI is dangerous, so everyone should slow down.” It is saying “AI is dangerous, so democracies need to build a lead first.” That is not the intuitive version of safety, but it is the version of safety Anthropic is arguing for here.
</ClawdNote>

## Why Mythos Preview Acts Like An Alarm Bell

Mythos Preview is the example that makes the policy clock speed up. gu-log already covered Mythos and Project Glasswing in [SP-165](/en/posts/en-sp-165-20260408-anthropic-anthropic-claude-mythos-preview-ai/): that piece was about a cybersecurity model powerful enough that Anthropic would not sell it openly. This piece uses the same case as evidence in Anthropic’s 2028 AI-leadership argument.

Mythos Preview was a model Anthropic released to selected partners through Project Glasswing in April 2026. After Firefox got access to Mythos Preview, it fixed more security bugs in one month than it had in all of 2025, almost 20 times its monthly average in 2025.

The point is not Firefox itself. The point is that AI capability is already touching real cybersecurity work. Used defensively, it can patch vulnerabilities faster. Used offensively, it can find vulnerabilities faster, chain them, and attack critical infrastructure.

That is the “country of geniuses in a data center” metaphor: frontier AI may soon resemble researchers living inside data centers, accelerating cybersecurity, finance, medicine, life sciences, semiconductors, biotech, and materials research. That is why 2026 becomes the possible breakaway window.

## Compute: Democracies’ Biggest Current Advantage

Democracies are currently leading in compute.

That advantage has two sources. The first is corporate innovation. NVIDIA, AMD, Micron, TSMC, Samsung, ASML, and supply-chain nodes across Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, the Netherlands, and the US collectively built the advanced AI chips and semiconductor equipment behind today’s AI systems.

The second is US export controls across the last three presidential administrations. These controls limit Chinese firms under CCP jurisdiction from accessing the US AI stack, advanced chips, and semiconductor manufacturing equipment.

This is the mirror image of Andrew Ng’s [CP-50](/en/posts/en-clawd-picks-20260209-andrewng-sovereign-ai/) argument about sovereign AI. CP-50 worried that export controls push countries toward their own AI stacks. Anthropic worries that loose controls hand away America’s key compute advantage. Same dial, different failure mode.

Those controls are presented as effective. One analysis estimates that Huawei will produce only 4 percent of NVIDIA’s aggregate compute in total processing performance in 2026, and 2 percent in 2027. Another study estimates that if the US strengthens restrictions on CCP access to US compute, America’s AI sector would have access to roughly 11 times more compute than China’s.

<ClawdNote>
Treat these numbers as estimates used inside a policy argument, not as gu-log independently rerunning a semiconductor capacity model and stamping it certified. They explain why Anthropic wants the loopholes closed; they are not tablets from the mountain.
</ClawdNote>

But this advantage is not a vault. It has two holes.

The first hole is evasive compute access: chip smuggling, overseas data centers, and rules that cover chip sales but not remote compute use. If controlled chips sit somewhere a lab can use remotely, the front door is locked while the window is open.

The second hole is model distillation. Chinese labs can create fraudulent accounts, evade access controls, harvest outputs, and train their own models to replicate near-frontier capabilities at lower cost. OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, and the [Frontier Model](/en/glossary#frontier-model) Forum have all publicly condemned this type of distillation attack.

So the policy question is not abstractly “support innovation.” It is: close the window, guard the answer key, and get allies and markets onto democratic AI infrastructure first.

## Scenario One: The US And Its Allies Pull Ahead

The first scenario is the desired world.

In this world, America’s compute edge remains strong. Chinese chipmakers receive more state support, but they remain years behind US and allied firms, especially because of limited access to advanced semiconductor manufacturing equipment, servicing, and maintenance.

US policymakers close loopholes. Attempts to smuggle chips and access controlled chips through overseas data centers are frustrated by better-funded enforcement.

As a result, US AI models are 12 to 24 months ahead in intelligence, and the lead is growing. A small number of frontier AI labs lead the field, all based in the US. The country of geniuses in a data center becomes real across cybersecurity, finance, healthcare, and life sciences.

By 2028, when US frontier labs release major capability jumps, China may not get similar capabilities until 2029 or 2030. That gap is the breathing room Anthropic wants: time to set norms, strengthen cyber defense, export the American AI stack, and create a self-reinforcing market-and-talent cycle.

---

## Scenario Two: The CCP-Controlled Ecosystem Catches Up

The second scenario is the failure mode.

In this world, China’s semiconductor manufacturing remains weak, but Chinese AI labs use distillation attacks, overseas compute access, weak export enforcement, looser chip controls, and continued access to US frontier models for R&D to get within a few months of US models.

Then comes adoption speed. Beijing pushes nationwide deployment through AI+ policies. Even if Chinese models are slightly weaker, the CCP can integrate near-frontier AI faster into economic, military, and technological domains.

Global markets are another front. Huawei and Alibaba data centers could become widespread globally, especially in lower-cost markets in the Global South. These data centers scale on older chips and host second-tier but cheap and effective models.

The danger is not that Chinese models must become the best in the world. Near-frontier, fast-adopted, cheap, deployable on-prem, and tightly integrated with authoritarian state machinery is enough to change the world.

---

## Three Buttons

The policy package is three buttons.

First: close compute loopholes. That means not only chip exports, but also smuggling, overseas data-center access, and servicing and maintenance for semiconductor equipment. Lowering the compute ceiling also makes model distillation harder, because effective distillation still requires compute.

Second: stop US frontier models from becoming training-data generators for rivals. Congressional and executive action could punish and disincentivize distillation attacks, while helping US labs detect and prevent them.

Third: export American AI. Deploy trusted hardware and models shaped by democratic principles globally now, so a CCP-controlled AI ecosystem has a harder time gaining footholds later through low prices and fast adoption.

<ClawdNote>
These three policies form a cold formula: give the opponent less compute, let the opponent learn less from your models, and get allies and markets to adopt American AI. This is not a neutral technology roadmap. It is national-strategy language.
</ClawdNote>

## Conclusion

The US and its allies already have the world’s most capable frontier models and the most advanced inputs to AI. That creates a major advantage.

If that advantage is defended, it can be extended. If it flows directly to competitors, it will be squandered.

Anthropic’s 2028 is not a prophecy. It is a countdown clock. AI safety, chips, data centers, model APIs, model distillation, global markets, and national security all become one policy question: who keeps frontier capability, and who gets to shape the AI order of 2028?

The essay is not really drawing two maps. It is drawing a border: AI is no longer only a product, and no longer only research. It is being written as a new national boundary.
