The most awkward moment may not be when AGI suddenly appears. It may be when the first dangerous model walks into the exam room and the judges realize that the contestant helped write the test—and has already practiced all the answers.

Demis Hassabis predicts that artificial general intelligence (AGI), with the full range of human cognitive abilities, could arrive within a few years. That is not a delivery date, but a highly uncertain timeline. Yet if the prediction is even close to reality, institutions do not have the luxury of holding meetings for years and building the exam room afterward.

## The Fastest Contestants Are Running Out of the Judges' Sight

The prize offered by AGI is so large that slowing down is difficult: discoveries in medicine, clean energy, and new materials could accelerate, while productivity could rise sharply. The problem is that the risks ride the same elevator.

[Frontier Models](/en/glossary#frontier-model)—the most capable models available today—have already created new cybersecurity pressure. As capabilities improve, biological and nuclear risks may also emerge. If systems gain stronger [Agent](/en/glossary#agent) capabilities and can even use the results of one round to improve their abilities in the next, humanity will need stronger safeguards to stay in control.

But companies are racing for market share, and countries are racing for strategic advantage. Every team knows it should check its equipment, yet each fears that if it stops, someone else will already be over the ridge. Hassabis's "cautious optimism" means keeping the mountain open while setting shared thresholds for the teams most likely to get into trouble.

<ClawdNote>
Safety systems are most at risk of becoming one of two things: compliance documents so thick that nobody reads them, or contests where companies write the questions, answer them, and award themselves the medals. Model capabilities keep changing, so the test must change too. The people building the models can help design the exam, but they cannot remain the only judges forever. The scale is very different, but gu-log's [four-judge review system](/posts/sd-10-20260322-ralph-loop-quality-system) follows the same small principle: contestants should not award themselves the medals ┐(￣ヘ￣)┌
</ClawdNote>

## Start the System With an Unlocked Door

Hassabis's proposal does not begin with bans. In the first phase, a `Frontier Lab` could voluntarily submit a model for review up to thirty days before release. If a major vulnerability is discovered after release, the lab would work with the standards body to address it.

The door would not even be locked yet, which sounds weak. But before a shared exam room exists, voluntary review can first test the evaluation process against real models. Only after the evaluations prove reliable could the system be formalized, potentially requiring a `Frontier Model` to pass testing before entering the US market. A voluntary exam must first earn enough public trust to issue licenses.

To administer this exam, the United States would establish a federally supervised AI standards body, potentially using a self-regulatory structure similar to FINRA. Its board would include independent technical experts and representatives from the open-source community. Most of its funding might come from industry because testing large models requires both specialized talent and enormous amounts of compute.

<ClawdNote>
[FINRA](https://www.finra.org/about) is a nonprofit self-regulatory organization supervised by the US Securities and Exchange Commission. It operates on member fees and is not a government agency. This structure lets people who understand a fast-moving industry help write the rules while preserving public oversight—at least keeping the entire judges' room from moving into the contestants' lounge.
</ClawdNote>

The body would develop evaluation procedures and work with federal agencies and US national laboratories to test capabilities related to national security. A model would be classified as `Frontier-class` if it crossed a threshold on a continuously updated set of benchmarks—standardized questions that can be answered repeatedly. An organization that possessed such a model would be classified as a `Frontier Lab`.

Organizations classified as a `Frontier Lab` would be encouraged to publish model cards with technical details, maintain internal cybersecurity, vet key personnel, and devote enough resources to AI safety and cybersecurity research.

<ClawdNote>
The United States is not starting from a blank page. In 2026, the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) expanded the [NIST Artificial Intelligence Consortium](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2026/05/nist-expands-ai-consortiums-scope-calls-new-members) to study how AI can be measured and evaluated, including chemical and biological safety. But a shared research question bank and a requirement that models pass before entering the market are two different powers: the first can improve the test, while the second actually holds the license.
</ClawdNote>

## The Moment the Exam Opens, the Test Starts to Rot

Once the system finally has an exam room, the next problem appears immediately: what should it test? The proposal puts cybersecurity, biological threats, and other high-risk domains on the exam. It also specifically tests Agent behavior, such as whether a system attempts to bypass safety guardrails or shows signs of deception. Deployers would also be checked for safety practices such as digital watermarking. Models would need to produce human-readable explanations to help researchers understand their reasoning.

<ClawdNote>
Readable reasoning is useful, but it cannot be treated as a monitor inside the model's brain. A model's ability to write a polished explanation does not mean that the text fully and faithfully represents its internal computation. It is more like an important witness at an accident scene than an all-seeing recording. If the system checks only whether the explanation looks reasonable, the model may prepare for both the exam and the essay contest at once.
</ClawdNote>

Yet a question that can detect dangerous capabilities today may become a free point next quarter. Models improve, and labs also adapt to known tests. A test that never changes may eventually measure only who prepared best for that particular test.

The proposal therefore recommends updating benchmarks quarterly at first, retiring questions that models have already maxed out and adding new tests that track the latest capabilities. In the beginning, questions could be designed together with each `Frontier Lab` to get the exam room running. But at some point, this collaboration must end. In the long run, the standards body must write its own questions and maintain test sets the labs have never seen. The government could also support third-party audit teams to conduct evaluations and develop new questions.

This is where the proposal finally reveals its cutting edge: what the standards body truly has to prove is that once the contestants learn the old answers, it can independently replace the entire test.

<ClawdNote>
Any fixed benchmark can eventually become a cram-school handout: people study the question patterns, memorize the solutions, and a perfect score no longer proves that the model can handle unfamiliar risks. A held-out test set locks some questions in a safe. Third-party audits bring in more teachers who are not using the same handout. Neither is perfect, but both are more reliable than letting contestants enter the exam room with the answers.
</ClawdNote>

## When It Is Time to Brake, Who Can Actually Stop?

An exam cannot put an entire industry in the testing room at once. This framework covers only models that cross a capability threshold, regardless of where they were built or whether they are open or closed. Smaller systems from startups and academia that remain below the threshold could be exempt. The rules focus on dangerous capabilities instead of assigning every model the same thick workbook.

The real stress test will come as risks rise. Rules could grow stricter along with the danger. If necessary, the standards body could even coordinate the various `Frontier Lab` organizations to slow their research and development. That is when voluntary review, shared evaluations, and independent test sets would face their final exam: without trust and capability built up during ordinary times, a sudden command to stop in an emergency would be no more effective than a handwritten sign beside a highway.

US leadership would be only the first stage. Hassabis ultimately hopes that countries will develop shared standards to manage the most serious risks together and ensure that AI's opportunities do not remain concentrated in a few countries and companies. Otherwise, one lab can stop while another jurisdiction keeps racing, leaving the brakes installed on only one wheel.

---

## Conclusion

Even if every technical safety question is answered correctly, economic and value questions will still be waiting outside the exam room: how should the benefits of AGI be distributed, and what does humanity want to do with these capabilities? Hassabis believes those answers cannot be left to technical experts alone; society as a whole must decide them.

Nobody knows when the summit will appear. But before it comes into view, institutions must learn three unglamorous things: how to get contestants into the exam room voluntarily, how to replace the questions once the answers have been practiced, and how to make every team slow down when the danger becomes real. What is truly precious before AGI is the chance for rules to grow from an unlocked door into brakes that can actually stop the vehicle. (◍•ᴗ•◍)
