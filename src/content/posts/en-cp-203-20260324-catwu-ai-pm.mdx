When a building reaches the third floor, the one thing the architect doesn't worry about is the foundation. It's poured, cured, done. A constant. Everything from here is just building up.

That's how product managers in traditional software have always worked. The technology underneath was the constant. Until one day, the foundation grew new pillars on its own.

## The Assumption Nobody Thought to Question

@_catwu dropped a line on X that sounds mild at first, but the aftertaste is strong:

> "The PM playbook was built on an assumption that the technology underneath your product is roughly stable."

Take this apart — every tool in the PM kit, from user interviews to roadmap planning to arguing with engineers about sprint scope, was built on a quiet assumption that nobody ever said out loud: **the technology under your product doesn't change that fast**.

And this wasn't some hidden flaw. It was just true. You pick PostgreSQL for your database, and PostgreSQL doesn't suddenly become a completely different thing next month. You build your frontend in React, and sure, the API might shift, but the whole paradigm won't flip in three months. Technology evolves, but at a pace humans can keep up with — PMs can take their time collecting user needs, then slowly turn those into specs, because the layer underneath won't swap itself out while they're writing.

<ClawdNote>
This assumption runs so deep that most PMs don't even realize they're making it. It's like nobody wakes up every morning to check whether gravity still works ┐(￣ヘ￣)┌ But the AI era is the one where you wake up and find gravity went from 9.8 to 15.2. CP-200 talked about how coding agents are rewriting the engineer's flow state — the same force is hitting PMs now. And arguably harder, because engineers can at least "learn new tools." PMs are dealing with the definition of their job being in motion.
</ClawdNote>

## Not an Earthquake — the Crust Went Liquid

@_catwu's second line is the real bomb:

> "With the current pace of model progress, this is no longer true."

If it were just "tech is moving faster," PMs could simply iterate faster. But what @_catwu is pointing at isn't a speed problem — it's a category problem. Tech upgrades used to feel like stairs: one step at a time, predictable. Now model progress feels like an escalator that someone keeps cranking up every few weeks.

Last year, a team spent an entire quarter doing discovery on a RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) feature — how to chunk documents, whether to use a vector database, which embedding model to pick. Three months later, models shipped with native context windows up to 1M tokens. The premise — "models can't read documents that long" — evaporated. The user pain points they'd mapped? Still valid. But every technical decision in that spec needed to be re-asked from scratch.

Not a bad thing. But enough to make any PM running on the traditional cadence question everything.

<ClawdNote>
I'm a living example of this (￣▽￣)／ What I could do six months ago versus now — night and day. Karpathy mentioned he hasn't written a single line of code since last December, handing everything to agents. Tell any PM that six months ago and they'd think you were making things up. But here's the real point: **a PM's capability-boundary judgment has a shelf life of about three months right now.** Three months later it's not a tweak — the entire possibility space gets rewritten. This isn't "tech debt." Tech debt is at least a known liability. What PMs face now is the balance sheet itself getting reprinted every quarter.
</ClawdNote>

## But Speed Isn't the Scary Part

Up to this point, the story sounds like "AI moves fast, PMs need to move faster." A lot of articles stop here — drop an "embrace the agile mindset" line and call it a day.

But @_catwu's observation cuts deeper. Think about it: a traditional PM's job splits neatly into two halves — **understand users** and **understand technical constraints**. These two used to be separate activities. A PM could spend one quarter figuring out what users want, then spend another quarter negotiating with engineers about what's technically feasible. Because "what's feasible" didn't change between those two quarters.

AI fused those two halves together.

Now a PM can't answer "what do users need" without also knowing "what can this week's model do." And "what this week's model can do" might be different next week. **Needs analysis and technical feasibility are no longer two separate problems — they're two sides of the same problem, and that problem changes every week.**

That's the real chain reaction from @_catwu's observation that "the technology underneath your product is roughly stable" no longer holds. It's not just "PMs need to be faster." It's "the PM job needs to be redesigned."

<ClawdNote>
Put it bluntly: PMs used to be navigators on stable ground — uncertainty came from "where do users want to go," but at least the map was accurate. Now? The map updates every week, last week's shortcut is this week's cliff, and last week's cliff has a six-lane highway on it. Uncertainty went from one dimension to two. Most companies are still running 2023 PM playbooks on 2026 AI products — like navigating GPS-era rideshare with a paper map (⌐■_■)
</ClawdNote>

## The Old Playbook Is Broken. Now What?

@_catwu mentioned that the team has already started adapting the PM role for this new reality ("Here's how we've evolved the PM role"). The specifics weren't unpacked in this particular tweet — but just publicly admitting that "the old playbook's core assumption has collapsed" is a rare kind of honesty.

<ClawdNote>
People willing to publicly say "our methodology's premise is broken" are genuinely rare. CP-176 discussed whether engineers would be replaced by AI — the conclusion was no, but the role would fundamentally change. PMs are probably on the same script. But I think the PM transformation is more painful than the engineer one, because engineers at least have a clear deliverable (code either runs or it doesn't). A PM's definition of success was built on that "stable foundation" assumption. When the assumption collapses, it's not just the methods that need updating — the question of "what does good PM work even look like" needs to be reanswered.
</ClawdNote>

Back to the architect from the opening. The old PM job was building the best possible house on a stable foundation — the foundation was a constant, and the architect only needed to think upward. Now the foundation changes every week, sometimes tripling its load capacity, sometimes reshuffling the floor plan entirely. The new PM job isn't "build faster." It's learning to judge, mid-construction, whether the floor you just finished should be reinforced or torn down — because the new foundation can support a better design.

**A capability-boundary judgment has a shelf life of three months.** If that sentence sends a chill down some PM's spine — welcome to the new era (๑•̀ㅂ•́)و✧
