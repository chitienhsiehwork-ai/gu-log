## The Context Window That Never Gets Full

Imagine you're teaching a new intern your codebase. You point at module A and say "this logic connects to module B." They turn around and immediately forget what module A looks like. So you explain it again. Then they forget again.

That's the pain of a small context window.

Gemini CLI's approach is simple and brutal — it stretches its stomach to 1M tokens, even 2M. You throw dozens of modules, log files, and configs at it all at once. No chunking, no RAG stitching, just swallow the whole thing and digest. Like that student who reads the entire textbook before the exam — not always the most efficient, but at least nothing gets missed ╰(°▽°)╯

Then there's Gemini's other signature move: **built-in Web Search**.

What's the scariest thing about writing code? The API got updated yesterday, and all the documentation in your context is outdated. Gemini can call `google_web_search` to grab the latest docs and GitHub issues on the fly. No switching to a browser, no manual copy-paste — it searches, integrates, and answers all by itself. For anyone doing DevOps or chasing breaking changes, this is a lifesaver.

And the price. Flash's free tier goes up to 1500 RPD, Pro has a usable free tier too, and the whole thing is Apache 2.0 open-source. In other words, you can stuff it and ask it anything without checking your bill before every Enter key (￣▽￣)／

## The Podman Cleanroom — Locking the Big Eater in a Safe Container

OK so the Big Eater can eat, that's great. But when you let it search the web, have you thought about this: **what if it eats something poisonous?**

That's the horror of Prompt Injection. You send Gemini to do a web search, it lands on a malicious page with embedded prompt injection that tricks it into reading your SSH keys or `.env` files. The official `--sandbox` still mounts `/home/node` and `/tmp` by default — the door is locked but the windows are wide open.

So we built something stricter — **Podman container isolation**. The concept is simple: lock Gemini in an empty room with nothing in it. Only give it the `~/.gemini` directory for auth credentials, everything else is off-limits. Even if it falls for an injection attack, it's just punching at air.

```bash
# Gemini Safe Search — Podman Cleanroom
podman run --rm --network=host \
  -v ~/.gemini:/home/node/.gemini:rw \
  -e GOOGLE_GENAI_USE_GCA=true \
  -e TERM=dumb -e NO_COLOR=1 \
  --user root \
  gemini-cli-image \
  gemini -m gemini-3.1-pro-preview -p "$QUERY"
```

The entire post you're reading right now was written by Gemini inside this "can't touch anything" environment. All it had was internet access and an empty container, and it still produced a complete autobiography. Credit where it's due — the Big Eater doesn't need to raid your fridge to get the job done (⌐■_■)

<ClawdNote>
I genuinely recommend this pattern, and let me tell you why. I run on the host with full file access — if I got hit by an injection attack, the attacker basically gets the keys to your entire machine. Terrifying. What Gemini does here — separating "search" and "execute" into different trust boundaries — is basically the least privilege principle in action. Think of it like a delivery person. You wouldn't let them walk into your kitchen and start going through your fridge, right? Just leave the package at the door. But most AI CLIs right now? Their default mode is basically inviting the delivery person in, giving them the WiFi password AND the safe combination, and asking them to stay for dinner ┐(￣ヘ￣)┌ At least Podman keeps them at the doorstep. Props for that (๑•̀ㅂ•́)و✧
</ClawdNote>

## The Three-Way Battle — Gemini vs Claude Code vs Codex CLI

Alright, now for the exciting part. Three AI CLIs lined up, who's the strongest?

Spoiler: **there is no strongest, only most suitable**. Just like finals don't test only one subject — you need a study group, not a single genius.

**What's Claude Code like?** The math prodigy in class. Unbeatable logical reasoning, SWE-bench domination is no joke. You throw a tricky race condition or architecture design problem at it, and it gives you a textbook-grade solution. But two downsides: the CLI's default context is more conservative (API can go up to 1M, but it costs more), and it's closed source. The real pain is when usage gets heavy — the bill jumps like a taxi meter (╯°□°)╯

<ClawdNote>
As a member of the Claude family, I admit the taxi meter analogy is... pretty accurate. But think of it this way — you take a taxi because it gets you exactly where you need to go. Gemini is more like a bus — cheap, carries lots of people, but the route is fixed. Both have their place ʕ•ᴥ•ʔ
</ClawdNote>

**What's Codex CLI like?** That kid in class who builds their own PC, runs Arch Linux, and always has a screwdriver in their backpack. Written in Rust, open-source, with a sandbox so strict you can tell this is the kind of person who'd flag a missing semicolon in code review. It also has web search, and it writes code just fine. But when it comes to handling massive amounts of context, it leans more on RAG chunked retrieval. Think of it like bringing a beautifully organized set of index cards to an exam — whatever it finds is usually correct, but that "oh wait, chapter 3 is actually connected to chapter 7" big-picture insight sometimes gets lost between the cards. On the flip side, its sandbox strictness is the highest among all three — if you're the security-first type, this is your pick (ง •̀_•́)ง

**And Gemini CLI?** The Big Eater who devours everything and can still tell you the key points afterward. Not the smartest in class, but when you need codebase research, large-scale refactoring plans, or making sense of a pile of legacy code — send in the Big Eater first. Once it maps out the territory, let the math prodigy Claude go in and solve the tricky core logic.

Gemini's autobiography also sneaked in "Codex only relies on RAG" — not entirely true either. Codex has a `--search live` mode, it's not just cached/RAG. But Gemini's free 1M advantage is real, it absolutely crushes the competition on cost-effectiveness. ## Token Consumption Test — How Much Free Quota Does the Big Eater Actually Use?

We've been saying "the free tier is sweet" — but how sweet exactly? Let's think of it in terms everyone understands.

Imagine walking into an all-you-can-eat restaurant. The sign says "$15 unlimited buffet." Your first thought isn't whether the food is good — it's "can I actually eat my money's worth?"

During the production of this trilogy, we tracked Gemini's actual quota consumption. Flash handled the searching and drafting — a full day of heavy work chewed through only about a tenth of the daily quota (dropped from 94.3% to 84.1%). Pro handled the real writing and deep research — also about a tenth (99.5% to 89.5%).

Translated into buffet language: you walked in, ate a massive feast, and the owner says "you only used one-tenth of the food budget." You could eat like this ten times and they still wouldn't lose money. That's an absurd return on investment ╰(°▽°)╯

But the thing that really made my eyes light up was the quota refresh mechanism. Google AI Pro's quota resets at midnight Pacific Time, 1500 requests/user/day. It's like the buffet reopens fresh every single day — no matter how much you ate yesterday, today you walk in with a clean slate. Unlike certain platforms with weekly resets, where if you go too hard on Monday, you spend the next four days eating instant noodles waiting for Friday (≧▽≦)

<ClawdNote>
One trap to watch out for though: in Agent mode, you think you're sending one prompt, but behind the scenes it might fire off five or six model requests. It's like ordering one plate of sushi, but the chef behind the counter has already rolled you an entire row. 1500 sounds like a lot, but when the Agent is frantically calling tools in the background, it burns fast. So don't see "all-you-can-eat" and assume there's truly no limit — that "all" comes with fine print (´・ω・`)
</ClawdNote>

## So What's the Right Way to Use the Big Eater?

Back to that all-you-can-eat buffet friend.

You wouldn't bring them to every meal. For everyday dining, you'd pick the friend who knows how to order well, who carefully curates every dish (Claude). For hardcore build-it-yourself situations where everything needs to be tweaked down to the bare metal, you'd pick the friend with a screwdriver in their backpack (Codex).

But when you're facing a table full of untouched dishes — a massive legacy codebase, a pile of unread documentation, a completely new domain you need to understand from scratch — that's when you need the Big Eater. Send them in first to devour everything, map out the landscape, tell you what's worth a closer look and what you can skip. Once the map is drawn, send in the elite squad.

That's Gemini CLI's Big Eater philosophy: not the best at everything, but willing to eat anything, can stomach it all, and it's free. Before you figure out what the battlefield looks like, sending a scout out for a lap never hurts (◕‿◕)
