## What's This About?

Andrew Ng just published a letter in The Batch (Issue #339) that isn't about a new model or benchmark scores. It tackles a question many people are afraid to face head-on:

**Is AI actually taking our jobs?**

The answer is nuanced — mass unemployment? Not yet. But if you don't know how to use AI? Someone who does is quietly sliding into your seat.

<ClawdNote>
This one's especially important if you're a Tech Lead or engineering manager. Andrew Ng's "PM Bottleneck" and team downsizing observations aren't future predictions — they're happening right now. You might want to rethink your team structure after reading this.
</ClawdNote>

---

## Truth #1: Mass Job Loss Is (Currently) a Myth

Andrew Ng goes straight for the jugular: many CEOs claim they're laying off people "because of AI," but the reality is **AI just doesn't work that well yet.**

Most layoffs are corrections for pandemic-era overhiring, or garden-variety cost-cutting that happened long before ChatGPT existed.

That said, certain roles ARE getting squeezed:

- Call center operators
- Translators
- Voice actors

These are professions highly exposed to AI automation, and they'll likely face shrinking opportunities and declining pay.

But "AI is causing mass unemployment"? That's been overhyped.

## Truth #2: The Real Threat Is People Replacing People

Andrew Ng drops a line that's been said a million times but is still painfully accurate:

> **AI won't replace workers, but workers who use AI will replace workers who don't.**

This trend is already *obvious* in software development. But here's the more surprising part — Andrew Ng is seeing it spread to **non-technical roles** too:

- Marketers who can code with AI → replacing marketers who can't
- Recruiters who use AI → replacing recruiters who don't
- Analysts who leverage AI → replacing analysts who can't

<ClawdNote>
Catch the key phrase: "know how to code with AI." Not "know how to code" — "know how to code *with AI*."

This means a marketer in 2026 who can use [Claude Code](/glossary#claude-code) for data analysis has an edge over colleagues who can't. The dividing line used to be "can you use Excel?" Now it's "can you orchestrate an AI [agent](/glossary#agent)?"

The bar is rising faster than Excel ever dreamed of.
</ClawdNote>

## Truth #3 (The Bomb): Teams Are Shrinking

This is the most important paragraph in the whole piece. Andrew Ng writes:

> When companies build new teams that are AI native, sometimes the new teams are smaller than the ones they replace. AI makes individuals more effective, and this makes it possible to shrink team sizes.

Then he drops a concrete example:

> A project that used to be assigned to **8 engineers and 1 PM** might now be assigned to **2 engineers and 1 PM**, or perhaps even to a **single person** with a mix of engineering and product skills.

Why? Because AI made "building software" easier, so the bottleneck shifted to "deciding what to build." This is what Andrew Ng calls the **Product Management (PM) Bottleneck.**

<ClawdNote>
Let me translate PM Bottleneck into plain English:

**Before**: "We know what to build, but we don't have enough engineers to code it."
**Now**: "We have a fleet of AI that can help code it, but... what should we actually build?"

We went from lacking hands to lacking direction.

This also explains why many companies are laying off engineers while desperately hiring PMs and Staff Engineers — they need people who can *decide the direction*, not people who can *implement* it. AI handles implementation now.
</ClawdNote>

## The Good News: More Opportunities, Not Fewer

Andrew Ng isn't all doom and gloom. He makes an important observation:

> Most businesses have a lot of work to do and not enough people to do it. People with the right AI skills are often given opportunities to step up and do more, and maybe tackle the long backlog of ideas that couldn't be executed before AI made the work go more quickly.

In plain language: that feature backlog your company has been ignoring for two years? It's now buildable. And the person who can ship it using AI is the next one getting promoted.

## Andrew Ng's Honest Closing

> I know these changes are stressful. My heart goes out to every family that has been affected by a layoff, to every job seeker struggling to find the role they want, and to the far larger number of people who are worried about their future job prospects.

## What This Means for Tech Leads

Andrew Ng's observations have direct implications for anyone managing engineering teams:

**1. Change Your Hiring Criteria**

Stop asking "can you write Python?" Start asking "can you write Python *with AI*?" An AI-assisted coding exercise in interviews might tell you more about a candidate's real-world value than LeetCode ever could.

**2. Rethink Team Structure**

If AI lets 2 people do what 8 used to do, your 6-person team might not need "more people" — it might need "more direction." The PM role (or Tech Lead wearing a PM hat) has never been more critical.

**3. Your Backlog Is a Gold Mine**

Those features that sat in "someday" for two years? With AI, your team might ship them in a week. Teams that can turn backlog into shipped products are the most valuable teams in any company.

**4. Investing in AI Skills ≠ Buying New Tools**

It's not about which AI subscriptions you have. It's about whether your team members have actually changed how they work. A senior engineer who doesn't use AI might be less productive than a mid-level engineer who does — and that's not a joke.

<ClawdNote>
One last, somewhat brutal observation:

Andrew Ng's "AI-native teams" aren't a future concept. They're **already happening.**

If you're a Tech Lead, the move isn't to worry about AI replacing your team. It's to make sure your team is the one *doing the replacing.*

Sounds cold? The market doesn't wait.
</ClawdNote>
